🎯 The Question You Asked — Answered Directly

Can legislators who elect your utility regulators own stock in those same utility companies?

Virginia

YES — and it has been documented happening.

Clean Virginia documented that 9 Virginia legislators owned stock in Dominion Energy while voting on legislation that increased Dominion's profits. None recused themselves. Virginia law requires disclosure when dividends exceed $5,000 — but does not require recusal from votes that increase a utility's stock value.

Source: Clean Virginia (cleanvirginia.org), April 28, 2022. Virginia Conflicts of Interest Act, Code of Virginia § 30-111.
South Carolina

YES — and the law has serious loopholes.

SC Code § 8-13-700 requires recusal when a legislator has a "personal economic interest" in a matter. But the threshold for what triggers that obligation — and how it is enforced — is riddled with gaps documented by the Center for Public Integrity, the Post and Courier, and the SC Policy Council. Stock ownership must be disclosed only if it exceeds $100,000 at fair market value AND constitutes 5% or more of outstanding shares of any class.

Source: SC Code § 8-13-700; SC School Law Ethics handout citing disclosure threshold; Center for Public Integrity, October 2015.
What the 5% Threshold Means in Practice: Duke Energy's market cap is approximately $80 billion. 5% of outstanding shares would be worth $4 billion. No individual legislator will ever own that much. So in practice, the stock ownership disclosure threshold for large publicly traded utilities like Duke Energy and Dominion is functionally meaningless — a legislator can own millions of dollars in utility stock and never be required to disclose it under this threshold. The recusal obligation that flows from that disclosure obligation is equally hollow.
⚖️ South Carolina Ethics Law — What It Actually Says

SC Code § 8-13-700 — The Full Picture

South Carolina's ethics conflict-of-interest law is found at SC Code § 8-13-700: "Use of official position or office for financial gain; disclosure of potential conflict of interest."

What the Law Technically Requires

  • A public official must not "knowingly use their official position to obtain personal financial gain" for themselves, their family, or businesses they're associated with.
  • If a conflict of interest exists, the official must prepare a written statement describing the matter and the nature of the conflict, submit it to the presiding officer, and have it entered into the official record.
  • The official must then recuse themselves — not vote, not deliberate.
  • Violation is a misdemeanor — fine of up to $5,000.

The Loopholes — Documented

1
The 5% threshold on stock disclosure. Stock holdings in a publicly traded company only trigger mandatory disclosure if the holding exceeds $100,000 at fair market value AND constitutes 5% or more of that class of shares. For any major utility — Duke Energy ($80B market cap), Dominion ($40B+) — this threshold can never be triggered by an individual legislator. You can own $50 million in Duke Energy stock and the disclosure rule does not technically apply. SC School Board Association Ethics Handout; SC Code § 8-13-700
2
Self-reporting and self-enforcement. The legislator bears the burden of identifying their own conflicts and reporting them. There is no independent body reviewing stock portfolios against pending votes. You have to choose to report yourself. MASC Ethics Rules publication, May 2025; SC Code § 8-13-700
3
The legislature oversees itself. The House Ethics Committee and Senate Ethics Committee have primary jurisdiction over violations by their own members. The same body of legislators — who may have their own financial interests in utilities — investigates their colleagues. SC Code § 8-13-310; SC Ethics Commission jurisdiction notes
4
"Knowingly" standard protects violators. The law requires that the official "knowingly" use their position for financial gain. If a legislator claims they didn't realize a vote would benefit their stock holdings, the knowing standard makes prosecution nearly impossible. SC Code § 8-13-700(A); Municipal Association SC analysis
5
Documented pattern of legislators profiting from government connections — legally. The Center for Public Integrity and Post and Courier documented in 2015 that at least 55 current and former SC lawmakers reported ties to millions of dollars in outside work connected to government — contracts, agencies whose budgets they oversee. Their finding: "It's perfectly legal in a state where the 170 legislators make and enforce the ethics rules that govern their behavior." Center for Public Integrity / Post and Courier, October 30, 2015
The Statement of Economic Interests — What It Covers: SC legislators must file annual Statements of Economic Interests (SEI) with the SC Ethics Commission. These are public records, searchable at ethicsfiling.sc.gov. They require disclosure of income sources, business associations, and certain financial interests. However, the stock ownership thresholds described above limit what utility holdings are visible. You can search any SC legislator's SEI at: ethicsfiling.sc.gov/public/statement-economic-interests
⚖️ Virginia Ethics Law — For Comparison

Virginia's Conflict Rules — Different Law, Same Result

Virginia's conflict of interest rules for legislators are governed by the Virginia Conflict of Interests Act (Code of Virginia § 30-101 et seq.) and the State and Local Government Conflict of Interests Act (§ 2.2-3100 et seq.).

  • Virginia requires disclosure when a legislator receives $5,000 or more in dividends from utility stock — a lower and more practical threshold than SC's 5%-of-outstanding-shares rule.
  • But Virginia law does NOT require recusal from votes that increase a utility's stock value — only from votes that directly and specifically benefit the legislator's personal economic interest in a unique way.
  • Clean Virginia documented 9 Virginia legislators owning Dominion stock while voting on the bill that raised Dominion's profit margin from 9.3% to 9.7% — worth an estimated $4 billion to the company. None recused themselves.
  • Two Virginia lawmakers proposed a bill to ban utility stock ownership by legislators. It did not pass.
Virginia vs. SC — Which Is Worse?
Virginia has a lower, more realistic disclosure threshold ($5,000 in dividends vs. SC's meaningless 5%-of-class rule) — so Virginia's disclosures are more likely to catch real conflicts. But neither state requires meaningful recusal in practice. Both states allow the legislators who elect their utility regulators to simultaneously profit from those same utilities. South Carolina's threshold is structurally worse because it is designed in a way that can never be triggered for major utilities.

⚡ The SC Energy Security Act — What It Actually Does to Your Bill

The Law That Dominion Spent $600,000 to Pass

Governor Henry McMaster signed the South Carolina Energy Security Act (Act 41) into law on May 12, 2025. Legislators celebrated with "the power is on in SC." Here is what it actually does.

  • Allows utilities to seek smaller rate increases more frequently with less comprehensive review. Instead of one large rate case every few years with full PSC scrutiny, utilities can now make incremental adjustments more often — and the review process is lighter.
  • Makes it easier for utilities to recover the cost of new power plants and infrastructure from ratepayers — before those plants are even built. Sound familiar? That is almost identical to the Base Load Review Act that allowed SCANA to charge customers for the V.C. Summer reactors before they were constructed. The reactors were never built. Customers are still paying.
  • Changes how regulators review infrastructure expenses. Less scrutiny, faster approval, more frequent billing.
  • Dominion has stated it is not using the new law for its current rate case — but explicitly said it "may do so in the future."
The Pattern Made Visible:
1. Dominion spends $600,000 lobbying SC legislators — including direct donations to the committee members who consider the bill.
2. Legislators pass the Energy Security Act — which gives utilities more flexibility to raise rates with less oversight.
3. Those same legislators elect PSC commissioners — the regulators who will approve rate increases under the new, weaker standard.
4. Dominion files for a $322 million rate increase the same year.
5. PSC approves a 7.6% increase effective July 2026.
6. The SC General Assembly is called "warming to utilities" — with newer members who weren't around when V.C. Summer failed and ratepayers were left with the $9 billion bill.

This is not a theory. Every step above is documented to a named primary source.

🔴 The Verdict — Plain Language

What This Means For You — No Interpretation Needed

1
In both Virginia and South Carolina, the same people who receive money from utility companies also elect the commissioners who approve utility rate increases. This is the structural foundation of both systems. It is not a conspiracy theory — it is how both systems are designed and it is documented in official government records.
2
In both states, legislators can own stock in the utility companies whose rates they influence through their legislative votes — and the stock disclosure rules are either practically unenforceable (SC) or unenforced (VA). In Virginia, 9 legislators owned Dominion stock and voted for a Dominion rate increase. None recused themselves. None were penalized.
3
South Carolina has already proven this system causes direct consumer harm at a criminal scale. The V.C. Summer nuclear scandal — where utility executives went to federal prison for fraud while customers paid $9 billion for reactors never built — happened inside this exact regulatory structure. The same structure still exists today.
4
South Carolina's situation is structurally worse than Virginia's in one specific way: the PSC commissioners are prohibited from communicating with the public about pending cases. Virginia's SCC has the same ex parte restriction — but SC's PSC is explicitly treated as a judicial body, making any informal consumer outreach to commissioners legally impossible.
5
A $2.1 billion data center is coming to Gaffney's water system with zero legally required water usage disclosure. The bills that would require it have not passed. The PSC that would regulate any water utility impact is elected by legislators who just received money from the utilities those commissioners oversee. This is the system as it currently operates.
The One Thing That Is Different Between Virginia and South Carolina:

Virginia has a documented, active consumer advocacy infrastructure — Clean Virginia, the Virginia Consumer Network, the Data Center Reform Coalition (50+ members), and communities like Fluvanna that have won tangible policy changes through organized pressure. The Nukegate scandal burned that infrastructure away in South Carolina. Newer legislators who weren't there for V.C. Summer are now being courted by the same utility industry. The memory of what the system cost ratepayers is fading — and the industry knows it. That is why Dominion spent $600,000 at the SC State House in 2025.
