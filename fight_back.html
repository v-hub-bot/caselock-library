1
The First Amendment Compelled Speech Argument
Constitutional — Federal Courts — Never Fully Litigated Against Utilities
UNTESTED AT SCALE STRONG CONSTITUTIONAL BASIS HARVARD LAW JOURNAL ARGUMENT

The Argument

When a state-approved utility rate includes costs for lobbying, political donations, and trade association dues — and you are legally required to pay that utility — the state is compelling you to fund private political speech you did not agree to. That is a First Amendment violation under the Supreme Court's 2018 ruling in Janus v. AFSCME.

The Janus Precedent

Janus v. AFSCME (2018): The Supreme Court held 5-4 that the government cannot compel individuals to subsidize a private organization's speech on matters of public concern. The case was about union dues — but the legal principle is identical. If you are forced to pay money that funds political speech you did not choose, your First Amendment rights are being violated.

How It Applies to Utilities

A Harvard Environmental and Energy Law Program legal fellow argued directly in a published Energy Law Journal article: "It is unconstitutional for state and federal utility regulators to authorize monopoly utilities' rates that include political activity costs." The argument:

  • You cannot choose a different utility — you are a captive ratepayer
  • The state approves the rate that includes political activity costs
  • You are legally required to pay that rate
  • Some of that rate funds lobbying, trade association dues (Edison Electric Institute), and political advertising
  • One audit of Ameren Corp found ratepayers were charged for the time employees spent working on company PACs
  • FERC audits routinely find utilities improperly charging lobbying costs above-the-line
  • Therefore: the state is compelling you to fund private political speech — unconstitutional under Janus
Why This Hasn't Been Litigated Yet: Utilities fight these cases ferociously at the discovery stage. Proving which costs in a rate are "political" requires access to internal accounting records. But after Janus, the constitutional hook is stronger than ever. A well-funded consumer organization or state attorney general could bring this case. The legal argument exists. The Supreme Court precedent exists. The case hasn't been made yet — which means the window is open.
⚡ Actionable Step
File a formal complaint at the SC PSC or VA SCC demanding an audit of all "above-the-line" rate expenses to identify political activity costs. Request itemization of trade association dues, lobbying expenses, and any PAC-related employee time charged to ratepayers. This forces the utility to produce records. Those records become the basis for a constitutional challenge.
Sources: Janus v. AFSCME, 585 U.S. ___ (2018); Eliza Martin, Harvard Environmental and Energy Law Program, Utility Dive, August 28, 2025; FERC audit findings documented by Energy & Policy Institute; Ameren Corp audit finding cited by Martin (2025).
2
Rate Case Challenge — Strip Political Costs from the Rate
Administrative Law — PSC/SCC Proceedings — Has Worked in Multiple States
PROVEN — HAS WORKED AVAILABLE RIGHT NOW

The Mechanism

In every utility rate case, the utility submits a cost-of-service study that itemizes every expense it wants to recover from ratepayers. Regulators are supposed to disallow any expense that is not "just and reasonable" or that constitutes a non-recoverable cost. Political expenses are supposed to be below-the-line — not recoverable from ratepayers. The problem is enforcement.

What Has Actually Worked

Six states have passed legislation that explicitly prohibits utilities from charging ratepayers for political activity. In rate cases in California, New York, and Oregon, intervenors have successfully challenged specific cost line items and had them removed from the recoverable rate base — saving ratepayers millions per case.

The California Precedent: California's CPUC has the most developed body of rate challenge case law. Intervenors in California have successfully argued for disallowance of specific lobbying expenses, executive compensation exceeding reasonable benchmarks, and trade association dues tied to political campaigns. The same legal arguments are available in every state — California just has more precedent because more people have tried.

How to Do This in SC or VA Right Now

  • File a formal petition to intervene in the current Dominion SC rate case (hearing scheduled May 12, 2026 before SC PSC, 101 Executive Center Drive, Columbia)
  • Submit discovery requests specifically asking for: all lobbying expense accounting, all trade association dues, any amounts paid to PACs or 501(c)(4) organizations, all above-the-line political expense classifications
  • Argue that any amount recovered from ratepayers for political activity is unlawfully included under SC Code § 58-27-810 (rates must be "just and reasonable") and unconstitutional under Janus
  • Even if you lose at the PSC level — you have built a record for appeal
Sources: Energy & Policy Institute, "Tracking State Legislation to Get Politics Out of Utility Bills" (2023-2025); NARUC "State Approaches to Intervenor Compensation"; California CPUC Intervenor Compensation Program (cpuc.ca.gov); SC Code § 58-27-810.
3
Formal Intervenor Status in Rate Cases
Administrative — PSC/SCC — The Most Underused Tool That Exists
AVAILABLE RIGHT NOW PROVEN RESULTS

What It Is

Any member of the public can petition to become a formal intervenor in a utility rate case. Once you have intervenor status, you are a legal party to the proceeding. You can subpoena documents. You can take depositions. You can submit expert testimony. You can cross-examine the utility's witnesses. You can appeal adverse decisions.

The Documented Returns

One California intervenor's opposition in a single rate case saved consumers $354 million. The intervenor received $784,000 in compensation for their work — paid by the utility. That is a 450-to-1 return for ratepayers. In another California case, a consumer intervenor's participation saved ratepayers billions over the life of the rate structure.

The Problem in SC and VA: Neither South Carolina nor Virginia has an intervenor compensation program — unlike California, Oregon, Hawaii, and 5 other states. In those states, if your intervention produces savings for ratepayers, the utility is required to compensate you for your time and costs. In SC and VA, you intervene at your own expense. That is a deliberate structural barrier to consumer participation. It is also a target for legislative reform — multiple consumer groups are pushing for intervenor compensation programs in both states.

4
Quo Warranto — Remove a Conflicted Official From Office
Ancient Common Law Writ — Available in Every State — Used Successfully in California
REQUIRES AG OR COURT PRECEDENT EXISTS

What It Is

Quo warranto is a legal writ — one of the oldest in common law — that literally means "by what authority." It asks: by what authority does this person hold this office? If a public official holds an office while in violation of a legal disqualification — including a conflict of interest — quo warranto can be used to remove them.

The California Precedent — A PSC Commissioner Was Removed

This is not theoretical. In 2000, the California Attorney General issued a formal opinion (Opinion No. 00-1005) addressing whether a California Public Utilities Commission member who purchased 700 shares of stock in a utility forfeited his office. The Foundation for Taxpayer and Consumer Rights filed a formal legal process with the California AG's office to initiate removal proceedings against CPUC Commissioner Henry Duque for exactly this conflict of interest. The legal basis was quo warranto.

How to Use This in SC or VA:
If you can document that a PSC commissioner or SCC commissioner holds financial interests in a regulated utility — through stock ownership, employment history, or other documented financial ties — you can petition the state Attorney General to initiate a quo warranto proceeding. You can also petition the court directly in most states. This requires documentation. But the tool exists and has been used.

In South Carolina: Petition the SC Attorney General's office.
In Virginia: Petition under Virginia Code § 8.01-636 (quo warranto proceedings).
Sources: California AG Opinion 00-1005 (oag.ca.gov); Consumer Watchdog (formerly Foundation for Taxpayer and Consumer Rights), "Consumer Group Begins Legal Process to Remove CPUC Commissioner Henry Duque" (consumerwatchdog.org); Virginia Code § 8.01-636; Wayne Law Review, "The Ultra Vires Doctrine: Evaluating Quo Warranto" (2021); Pennsylvania Law Review on quo warranto standards.
5
State Attorney General — Consumer Protection Enforcement
Consumer Fraud Law — Documented AG Actions Have Won Hundreds of Millions
HAS PRODUCED WINS REQUIRES POLITICAL WILL

The Authority

Both the Virginia AG (under the VCPA — Virginia Consumer Protection Act, Code of Virginia § 59.1-196 et seq.) and the South Carolina AG (under SC Unfair Trade Practices Act, SC Code § 39-5-10 et seq.) have authority to investigate and pursue enforcement actions against entities engaged in unfair, deceptive, or fraudulent business practices. Utility companies are not automatically exempt.

South Carolina — The SCE&G Case (Andrade v. Johnson)

The South Carolina Supreme Court addressed whether SCE&G (now Dominion Energy SC) was exempt from the SC Unfair Trade Practices Act. The Court of Appeals reversed the trial court's finding that SCE&G was exempt from the UTPA. The SC Supreme Court affirmed. This is on the books: in South Carolina, a utility company is not automatically shielded from consumer protection law claims. The door is open.

What the AG Can Do

  • Investigate deceptive billing practices — if a utility charges you fees that were never disclosed, that is a UTPA/VCPA violation
  • Demand document production without a civil lawsuit — AG investigative subpoenas are powerful
  • File for injunctive relief to stop a billing practice while investigation proceeds
  • Seek restitution for all affected ratepayers — effectively a class-wide remedy without a class action
  • Refer findings to the PSC/SCC as evidence in rate proceedings
The Problem in Virginia — Class Action Was Just Vetoed: Virginia has long been one of only two states in the entire country without a state-level class action procedure. In 2026, the Virginia legislature passed SB229/HB449 — which would have finally created it. Governor Spanberger vetoed it on May 19, 2026. Virginia businesses lobbied hard for the veto. This means individual Virginia consumers must pursue individual cases or rely on the AG — there is no mechanism to aggregate claims in state court. The AG route is therefore even more important in Virginia specifically.
⚡ File a Complaint With the AG Right Now
Virginia AG: ag.virginia.gov — consumer protection complaint form
South Carolina AG: scag.gov — consumer protection division
Document everything: billing changes, rate notices, advisory warnings, dates, amounts. The more complaints the AG receives about the same utility, the more likely they are to investigate. Individual complaints aggregate into an enforcement picture.
Sources: Andrade v. Johnson, SC Supreme Court (2003) — law.justia.com/cases/south-carolina/supreme-court/2003/25738.html; SC Unfair Trade Practices Act § 39-5-10; Virginia Consumer Protection Act § 59.1-196; McGuireWoods, "Virginia's Class Action Bill Killed," May 19, 2026; Citizens Utility Board examples of AG enforcement producing ratepayer refunds.

FOIA — Freedom of Information Act Requests
State and Federal — The Foundation for Every Other Fight
AVAILABLE TO ANYONE FREE TO FILE

What FOIA Gets You

FOIA requests to government agencies — the PSC, the SCC, the ORS, the SCDES, county government — can produce the actual documents the utilities submitted, the actual agreements signed, the actual lobbying disclosures, and the actual internal communications between regulators and utilities. These documents become the evidence base for every other fight.

Specific Targets in SC Right Now

  • Cherokee County / Cielo data center: Any water supply agreement between Cherokee County, Gaffney BPW, and Cielo Digital Infrastructure — FOIA Cherokee County government and the BPW
  • Dominion SC lobbying records: Full public records of all lobbying disclosures filed with the SC Ethics Commission — already public, just not aggregated. Use ethicsfiling.sc.gov
  • PSC internal communications: Request all communications between PSC commissioners and Dominion representatives during the current rate case proceeding — these are government records
  • BPW water capacity assessment: The assessment the BPW conducted before telling city leaders infrastructure was "sufficient" for data center use — request the full report
⚡ SC FOIA — How to File
SC Freedom of Information Act: SC Code § 30-4-10 et seq.
Address to the custodian of records at the agency. State exactly what you want. Give them 10 business days (mandatory response window).
Template: "Pursuant to the South Carolina Freedom of Information Act (SC Code § 30-4-10 et seq.), I hereby request all documents, agreements, emails, and communications relating to [specific subject] from [date range]."
If they deny — appeal within 30 days. The SC Press Association has a free FOIA hotline: 803-750-9561.
Sources: SC Freedom of Information Act, SC Code § 30-4-30 (10 business day response requirement); Virginia FOIA, Virginia Code § 2.2-3700; SC Press Association FOIA resources.
7
Federal Intervention — EPA, FERC, and CFPB
Federal Agencies — Multiple Jurisdictions — Bypasses State Capture
BYPASSES STATE REGULATORS REQUIRES DOCUMENTED VIOLATIONS

EPA Safe Drinking Water Act Enforcement

If a public water utility — including Gaffney BPW or Aqua Virginia — violates any federal drinking water standard, EPA has enforcement authority that bypasses the state PSC entirely. In FY2025, EPA completed 419 SDWA enforcement actions. You do not need the PSC or the SCC to get EPA involved. You need documented violations.

  • File a formal complaint with EPA Region 4 (Atlanta — covers South Carolina): epa.gov/aboutepa/epa-region-4-southeast
  • File a formal complaint with EPA Region 3 (Philadelphia — covers Virginia): epa.gov/aboutepa/epa-region-3-mid-atlantic
  • Request EPA inspection of your water system
  • Citizen suits under the Safe Drinking Water Act: 42 U.S.C. § 300j-8 — allows private citizens to sue violators directly in federal court

FERC — Federal Energy Regulatory Commission

FERC has jurisdiction over wholesale electricity rates and transmission. While retail rates are state-regulated, FERC's audit authority extends to how utilities account for expenses — including whether lobbying and political expenses are being improperly classified and recovered from ratepayers. FERC audits have repeatedly found exactly this violation. File a complaint at: ferc.gov/industries-data/electric/electric-power-markets/complaints

The Clean Water Act Citizen Suit

The Clean Water Act (33 U.S.C. § 1365) authorizes any citizen to file a civil lawsuit against any person — including a utility — for violating a discharge permit or effluent standard. The suit goes directly to federal district court. No state regulator involvement required. The Guide to Clean Water Act Citizen Suits (California State Water Board) is the best public resource for how to proceed.

Sources: EPA SDWA enforcement statistics FY2025; 42 U.S.C. § 300j-8 (SDWA citizen suits); 33 U.S.C. § 1365 (Clean Water Act citizen suits); FERC complaint procedures; California State Water Board Clean Water Act Citizen Suits guide.

Federal Class Action — The Path Virginia Just Closed at State Level
Federal Court — Available Despite Virginia's State-Level Veto
AVAILABLE IN FEDERAL COURT REQUIRES FEDERAL HOOK

Virginia Just Vetoed State Class Actions — But Federal Court Is Still Open

Governor Spanberger vetoed Virginia's class action bill on May 19, 2026 — making Virginia one of only two states still without a state-level class action procedure. But federal class actions under Fed. R. Civ. P. 23 are available for federal law claims regardless of what Virginia does at the state level. If the claim involves a federal statute — TILA, FDCPA, FCRA, SDWA, Clean Water Act — federal class action is available.

South Carolina — Class Action IS Available in Federal Court

South Carolina courts have addressed UTPA class action questions. For federal claims, federal class action under Rule 23 is fully available in the District of South Carolina. The Andrade v. Johnson precedent confirms SC utilities are not automatically shielded from consumer protection claims.

The Aqua Virginia / Essential Utilities Angle

If Essential Utilities / American Water merger proceeds — federal antitrust law (Sherman Act, Clayton Act) may provide a federal hook for a class action challenging the merger's effects on ratepayers. The FTC and DOJ Antitrust Division have jurisdiction over mergers that substantially lessen competition. A merger that eliminates a competing water utility and creates a larger monopoly with demonstrated rate increase patterns is a textbook Clayton Act Section 7 challenge.

Sources: Fed. R. Civ. P. 23 (federal class action procedure); McGuireWoods analysis of Virginia veto, May 19, 2026; Andrade v. SC Supreme Court 2003; Clayton Act § 7 (15 U.S.C. § 18) on anticompetitive mergers; Citizens' Utility Board successful federal class action examples.
9
Push for Intervenor Compensation Programs
Legislative Reform — Already Works in 8 States — Changes Everything
REQUIRES LEGISLATION PROVEN MODEL

What It Is and Why It Changes the Math

California, Oregon, Hawaii, and five other states require utilities to pay the legal costs of consumers who successfully intervene in rate cases. One California intervenor spent $784,000 and saved ratepayers $354 million. When consumers get paid for fighting, they fight. Without compensation, only wealthy organizations can afford to intervene. That is not an accident.

The Legislative Ask — Simple and Specific

Ask your SC state representative and senator to introduce a bill modeled on California Public Utilities Code § 1801.3 — the intervenor compensation statute. The bill requires one thing: if a consumer group's participation in a rate case produces demonstrable savings for ratepayers, the utility must compensate that group's reasonable costs. It does not cost the state a dollar. It comes from the utility. It has passed in 8 states. It can pass in South Carolina.

Sources: California Public Utilities Code § 1801.3 (intervenor compensation statute); CPUC Intervenor Compensation Program annual reports; OC Register, April 19, 2024; NARUC intervenor compensation survey; Canary Media, "Intervenor Compensation Programs Can Level the Regulatory Playing Field."
10
Ballot Initiative — Take the Vote Away from the Legislature
Direct Democracy — Has Worked in Multiple States — The Nuclear Option
REQUIRES MASS SIGNATURES BYPASSES LEGISLATURE ENTIRELY

What It Does

Both Virginia and South Carolina have citizen initiative and referendum processes. A ballot initiative can amend state law — or even the state constitution — directly, without going through the legislature that the utilities fund. This is the nuclear option. It is hard. It requires massive organizing. And it has worked.

Ohio — Voters Repealed the FirstEnergy Bailout

After the Ohio legislature passed HB6 — the FirstEnergy-funded nuclear plant bailout that cost ratepayers billions — consumer advocates launched a ballot referendum to repeal it. Even though FirstEnergy spent millions to block the referendum (including criminal acts to prevent the referendum from reaching the ballot), the effort showed that direct democracy can override utility-captured legislatures. The criminal prosecution ultimately voided the legislation anyway.

What a SC Ballot Initiative Could Do

  • Prohibit investor-owned utilities from making political contributions to state candidates or parties — reinstating the pre-2005 PUHCA protection at the state level
  • Require popular election of PSC commissioners instead of General Assembly election
  • Mandate an intervenor compensation program
  • Require real-time public disclosure of utility lobbying expenditures
Sources: Ohio HB6 referendum effort; FirstEnergy criminal prosecution; South Carolina initiative and referendum procedures (SC Constitution Article III); Virginia referendum procedures (Virginia Code § 24.2-681).
