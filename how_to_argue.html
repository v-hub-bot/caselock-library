🎯 The Core Legal Argument — The One That Survives

The Strongest Version — Credit to Community Advocate Eileen Barry Moore, Verified Against Zoning Law

The Argument in One Paragraph
"Data centers are a uniquely burdensome land use that impose disproportionate infrastructure, environmental, and community costs relative to their local benefit — and because of that, they should be restricted to areas specifically designed to absorb those impacts."

Why this works legally: It does not argue the industry shouldn't exist. It argues that this type of land use, in this location, fails the standard tests for zoning approval — compatibility with surrounding uses, proportionate burden on public infrastructure, and adequate mitigation of impacts on existing residents. Every zoning code in the country has these tests. This argument speaks directly to them.

This is also the argument Virginia's SB 94 / HB 153 (Danica Roem / Josh Thomas) is built on — that facilities drawing 100MW+ of electricity must assess noise, water, agricultural, historic, and park impacts within 500 feet. The community argument and the legislative argument are aligned.

📋 Five Pillars — Each With Verified Evidence Behind It

Build Each Argument With a Source You Can Hand to the Board

1
Massive Grid Impact, Disproportionate to Local Return
A single hyperscale AI data center draws 100,000+ homes worth of electricity (Lincoln Institute / IEA). Virginia's Dominion Energy has contracted 47 gigawatts of data center load — nearly tripling peak capacity — without a public vote (PEC Virginia, Jan 2025). The Mid-Atlantic power capacity auction jumped from $2.2B to $14.7B in one year — that cost falls on all ratepayers (California SB 57, Oct 2025). Oklahoma passed ratepayer protection unanimously. Virginia has not acted. Hand the board the JLARC 2024 report.
2
Water Consumption That Threatens Shared Public Resources
Virginia data centers consumed 2.1 billion gallons in 2023 — an 86% increase since 2019 (JLARC Rpt598). Caroline County's 13.9 MGD Rappahannock permit was suspended after public opposition because the same permit was needed for drinking water. Bowling Green wells show gross alpha radioactive contamination at 26-40 pCi/L — the limit is 15 (Fredericksburg Free Press, Nov 2024). Google's Council Bluffs Iowa data center alone used 1.3 billion gallons in 2024 — 3.7 million gallons per day (NASUCA 2025). Ask for a cumulative water impact assessment before any vote.
3
Noise, Air Quality, and Quality-of-Life Impacts on Adjacent Residents
24/7 industrial HVAC systems produce chronic low-frequency noise that penetrates residential walls. Diesel backup generators release particulate matter, NOx, and carbon monoxide during testing (weekly or monthly) and during actual outages. Virginia DEQ has permitted thousands of diesel generators for data centers (PEC, Jan 2025). The original post: "Older people with health issues and families with children who have asthma will have to move." This is not rhetorical — it is a documented pattern from communities near existing facilities. Request air quality modeling for generator testing events specifically.
4
Property Value Impact on Adjacent Landowners
Massive windowless concrete boxes, high-security fencing, barbed wire, and large electrical substations fundamentally alter neighborhood character. Community testimony across multiple states (Hanover VA, Festus MO, Hancock County IN, Morrow County OR) documents residents unable to sell homes near data centers. This argument is supported by standard real estate impact analysis — the same type required for other large industrial uses. Commission an independent property value impact study as a condition of approval.
5
Jobs Promised vs. Jobs Delivered — The Core Economic Lie
Hyperscale data centers employ 30-50 permanent workers per facility while drawing power equivalent to 100,000 homes. Conventional data centers employ more but still far less than promised. Sen. Richard Stuart (R-Westmoreland): "I cannot, for the life of me, explain how a data center is good economic development for our region because they don't create any jobs." Tax revenue takes 5-10 years to materialize through performance agreements. CRG Clayco's Festus MO project was described by the city administrator as "a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity" — voters threw out every incumbent council member on the ballot who approved it — 4 of the 6 yes votes were up for election, all 4 lost (St. Louis Public Radio, April 8, 2026; Politico, April 13, 2026). Demand a jobs impact analysis using actual comparable facility data, not projections.
All figures sourced from: JLARC Rpt598; PEC Four Pillars; Lincoln Institute; NASUCA 2025; California SB 57; KCUR April 9, 2026; Fredericksburg Free Press Nov 2024; Sen. Stuart quotes November 2024.
🎤 Hearing Scripts — Word-for-Word, Built to Survive Legal Challenge

Three Minutes at the Microphone. Use This.

Opening Statement — 90 Seconds
"My name is [name]. I am a resident of [address] and a property owner/taxpayer/voter in this jurisdiction. I am not here to say data centers should not exist anywhere. I am here to say that this facility, in this location, fails the standard tests for zoning approval that this board is required to apply.

A hyperscale AI data center is not comparable to a conventional office or light industrial use. It draws the electricity of 100,000 homes. It consumes millions of gallons of water per day from a shared public supply. It runs diesel generators — tested weekly — that release particulate matter and nitrogen oxides. It operates 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, generating constant low-frequency noise that penetrates residential walls.

I am asking this board to require a full cumulative impact assessment before any vote — including water consumption, air quality during generator testing, noise modeling at residential property lines, and property value impact on adjacent landowners. That is what the evidence warrants and what the law requires."
The Jobs Challenge — 60 Seconds
"I want to address the jobs argument directly. Hyperscale data centers employ 30 to 50 permanent workers per facility. This is documented by industry sources and confirmed by the Virginia Joint Legislative Audit and Review Commission's 2024 report. In exchange for 30-50 jobs, this facility will draw the electricity equivalent of [X] thousand homes, consume [X] million gallons of water per day from our shared supply, and permanently alter the character of [neighborhood]. I am asking the board to require the applicant to submit verified employment data from comparable operating facilities — not projections — before this vote proceeds."
The Water Argument — 60 Seconds
"Virginia data centers consumed 2.1 billion gallons of water in 2023 — an 86% increase in four years. This is not an activist number. This is from the Virginia Joint Legislative Audit and Review Commission's official 2024 report. In Caroline County, the county's application to withdraw 13.9 million gallons per day from the Rappahannock River was suspended by the DEQ because the same permit was required for residential drinking water. The wells in Bowling Green already show radioactive contamination above the legal limit. I am asking this board to require a disclosure of the specific water volume this facility will consume per day, per month, and per year — and a cumulative impact assessment covering all data center facilities in this watershed — before any approval is granted."
The Closing — 30 Seconds
"One week after the Festus, Missouri city council approved a $6 billion data center over community objection, voters threw out every council member who voted for it. That was April 2026. The community of Cascade Locks, Oregon recalled their port authority officials and cancelled the project entirely. Warrenton, Virginia voted out every council member who supported Amazon's proposal. This board has a choice. The community is watching, and the community will remember."
🚫 What NOT to Say — The Arguments That Lose
❌ These arguments lose — every time
"They shouldn't exist" — crushed immediately by property rights and economic development arguments
"This is a surveillance state" — valid concern, but not a zoning argument; gets you dismissed as a conspiracy theorist
"They're poisoning us" without attribution — exposes you to defamation risk; the Jennifer Combs case in Texas is the cautionary tale
"Big Tech is evil" — editorial; not a zoning standard; gives the board cover to dismiss you
Unverified statistics — if you cite a number that gets challenged and you can't source it, you lose credibility on everything
"Everyone in town is against this" — anecdotal; the board can dismiss it; bring a petition with actual signatures
✅ These arguments win — sourced and legally durable
"Disproportionate burden on shared public infrastructure" — the legal standard that wins in zoning
"Cumulative impact has not been assessed" — procedural argument; very hard to override
"Comparable facility data shows [X] jobs, not [Y]" — forces applicant to defend their projections
"Water disclosure was not provided — SB 553/HB 496 requires this" — legislative authority
"Property value impact on adjacent landowners has not been studied" — standard in industrial siting reviews
"Adjacent residents with asthma and respiratory conditions will face documented air quality impacts from generator testing" — specific, medical, attributed
📁 Building the Record Before the Hearing

What to Gather. What to Submit. What to FOIA.

  • FOIA the application file: Every permit application has supporting documents — traffic studies, environmental assessments, water impact analyses. Request them all before the hearing. Virginia Code § 2.2-3704. Response required within 5 business days.
  • FOIA the water commitment: What volume has the applicant committed to consuming? This should be in the application. If it's not there — that's the argument: "No water volume disclosure was provided."
  • Look up the LLC: Virginia SCC Clerk's Information System (scc.virginia.gov/clk). Find who is really behind the project name. Know your opponent before you walk in.
  • Get the noise ordinance: Every locality has a noise ordinance with decibel limits at property lines. Ask a professional engineer to assess whether the proposed facility can comply. If no noise study was submitted with the application — that's the argument.
  • Bring a petition: Not just as evidence of community opposition — but as a formal document submitted into the record. Every name on that petition is a documented objector. The board cannot pretend the opposition doesn't exist.
  • Coordinate with neighboring property owners: People within 500 feet of the proposed facility have standing to demand direct notice of hearings. Make sure they all know and all attend.
  • Record the hearing: Virginia public meetings are recordable by law. Recording protects you if the board mischaracterizes what was said. It also creates the record for appeal.
🗳️ After the Hearing — When the Vote Goes Wrong

The Hearing Is Not the End. These Are Your Next Steps.

  • If they approve it: You have 30 days to appeal most local zoning decisions in Virginia to the circuit court. Contact a Virginia land use attorney immediately. The appeal must be filed within the statutory window.
  • Document every official's vote: Name, district, vote. This goes on record for the next election. Festus, Missouri and Warrenton, Virginia both show that one election cycle changes everything.
  • Start the recall process if applicable: 39 states allow citizen-driven recalls (KimmRussel community documentation). Virginia does allow recall petitions under Va. Code § 24.2-233 for officers guilty of misfeasance, malfeasance, or neglect of duty. Consult an attorney on whether the approval qualifies.
  • Contact the Data Center Reform Coalition: pecva.org/datacenters — they have legal resources and coordinate statewide strategy. Your local fight is part of a larger documented pattern they can help you leverage.
  • File with the SCC: If the project requires utility infrastructure commitments, those go through the SCC. File to intervene in any related SCC proceeding. This is a separate legal avenue from the local zoning fight.
Virginia recall statute: Va. Code § 24.2-233. Appeal window: Va. Code § 15.2-2285 (30-day window for zoning decisions). Recall state count: community documentation (KimmRussel). For legal action, consult a licensed Virginia attorney.
