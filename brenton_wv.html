The Law — What's Being Violated

Federal and State Law These Companies Are Breaking

⚖️ Clean Water Act (CWA) — 33 U.S.C. § 1251 et seq.
  • Prohibits discharge of pollutants into navigable waters without a valid NPDES permit
  • Lexington Coal: 186 documented violations of its own permit since October 2021. EPA-confirmed Significant/Category I Noncompliance. This is a textbook CWA enforcement case.
  • Rockwell Mining: alleged unpermitted discharge at Kopperston — no NPDES permit, confirmed contaminated outfall flowing into the Guyandotte River system
  • Citizen suit provision (§ 1365): Any person may file suit directly in U.S. District Court after a 60-day notice of intent. Appalachian Mountain Advocates filed 60-day notices Aug. 29, 2025. Litigation window opened Oct. 28, 2025.
⚖️ Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act (SMCRA) — 30 U.S.C. § 1201 et seq.
  • Requires mine operators to restore land and water to pre-mining conditions
  • Requires operators to control drainage and prevent water pollution from mine runoff
  • Both the Kopperston and Lynco notices allege SMCRA violations alongside CWA violations
  • Bankrupt companies do NOT escape SMCRA obligations — courts have held that reclamation duties survive bankruptcy and transfer to acquiring companies
⚖️ Endangered Species Act (ESA) — 16 U.S.C. § 1531 et seq.
  • The Guyandotte River is designated critical habitat for the federally endangered Guyandotte River Crayfish
  • Both the Kopperston and Lynco discharges flow into tributaries that feed the Guyandotte
  • Testing showed pH 4.7 and heavy metal levels at Kopperston — at these levels, "there are negative impacts to the small streams where the discharge is going in directly" — confirmed by Appalachian Voices scientist Matt Hepler, Aug. 2025
  • Federal agencies must ensure their actions are not likely to jeopardize listed species. DEP's approval of mine expansions may independently violate the ESA.
⚖️ Safe Drinking Water Act (SDWA) — 42 U.S.C. § 300f et seq.
  • A 2025 peer-reviewed study published in Risk Analysis ranked Wyoming County among the 10 worst counties in the entire United States for Safe Drinking Water Act violations — two Wyoming County public water systems had the highest number of drinking water violations recorded for any single system during the study period. (West Virginia Rivers Coalition; Risk Analysis journal 2025)
  • The Guyandotte River is used as a source for the local drinking water supply — contamination of tributaries directly threatens public water infrastructure
⚖️ West Virginia Water Pollution Control Act — W.Va. Code § 22-11
  • Prohibits discharge of any substance that causes water quality to violate state standards
  • The DEP's own sampling has documented violations — yet enforcement actions have been systematically dismissed or delayed
What's In the Water — Verified Testing Data

The Science. Not Opinions. Not Fears. Documented Test Results.

🔬 Kopperston Site — Sampled June 24, 2025 (Appalachian Voices)
  • pH: 4.7 — highly acidic. Normal drinking water is 6.5–8.5. Below 6.5 is corrosive and toxic to aquatic life.
  • Aluminum: 5.42 mg/L — EPA MCL for aluminum in drinking water: 0.05–0.2 mg/L. This is 27–108× the safe limit.
  • Iron: 2.78 mg/L — EPA secondary standard: 0.3 mg/L. This is 9× the standard.
  • Selenium: ~2.0 mg/L (estimated) — EPA MCL: 0.05 mg/L. This is 40× the safe limit. Selenium toxicity causes hair loss, nerve damage, and liver damage.
  • Manganese: 0.710 mg/L — EPA health advisory: 0.3 mg/L. Manganese at elevated levels causes neurological damage, particularly in children.

⚖️ Federal Methane Regulation — Clean Air Act 2024 EPA Rule

While methane is not classified as a hazardous air pollutant under the CAA, it is regulated under the Act. As of 2024, the EPA adopted stricter methane guidelines. Uncontrolled methane venting from mine-related sources may independently trigger federal CAA enforcement obligations. Communities should document every vent point with GPS coordinates and video.

🔬 What Data Centers Actually Do to Water — Verified Science
  • Data centers do NOT use "closed loop" cooling — evaporative cooling towers expel 80–90% of water permanently into the atmosphere. It does not return to the local aquifer. (EESI 2024; Tammy Clark testimony, MI House Oversight Committee, Jan. 28, 2026)
  • A single large data center uses up to 5 million gallons per day — equivalent to a town of 30,000–50,000 people (Lincoln Institute of Land Policy 2024)
  • Cooling system discharge contains: anti-corrosives, biocides, hexavalent chromium, and other industrial chemicals that can enter groundwater if discharge is improperly handled
  • In Morgan County, Georgia: The Morris family, 400 feet from Meta's data center, has had brown tap water since 2018. Rep. Ocasio-Cortez brought it to Congress on May 20, 2026 and demanded an EPA investigation. Same companies. Same infrastructure. Now coming to WV coalfields.

Federal Rollback — Making It Worse

Trump EPA Just Rolled Back Mercury Protections — Right Now — Coal States Are First Hit

🚨 FEBRUARY 19, 2026 — EPA FINALIZED MATS RULE REPEAL

On February 19, 2026, the Trump EPA finalized the repeal of the 2024 amendments to the Mercury and Air Toxics Standards (MATS) Rule. This removed:

  • A mandatory 70% reduction in mercury emissions from plants burning lignite coal
  • A mandatory 67% reduction in mercury from other coal plant types
  • Requirements that coal power plants treat toxic wastewater containing arsenic, mercury, and heavy metals before discharge

West Virginia is coal country. This rollback means more mercury, arsenic, and heavy metals are now legally allowed to enter air, soil, and water in Wyoming County and across the coalfields. Combined with already-contaminated mine water flooding the aquifer, this is a compounding crisis.

Source: EPA MATS Final Rule Feb. 19, 2026 · Earthjustice press release 2026 · E&E News · The New Lede Feb. 2026

Action Steps

What Residents, Advocates, and Attorneys Can Do Right Now

📸 Step 1 — Document Everything — Today
  • Video your tap water every single time it is discolored, smells, or bubbles — date and time stamp it
  • If you can smell gas from a well or ground pipe — video it, note GPS coordinates, call 911 AND DEP AND document that you called
  • Photograph any dead animals, discolored streams, or foam on water
  • Save every utility bill, filtration receipt, and water purchase receipt — these are your damages
  • Get your well water tested independently — not by the company, not by the agency. Contact Appalachian Voices or Mountain Water Watch for guidance
📋 Step 2 — File Formal Complaints — On the Record
  • WVDEP: 1-800-642-3074 — every complaint creates a dated paper trail even if no action is taken
  • EPA Region 3 (covers WV): r3.public@epa.gov — formal Clean Water Act citizen complaint
  • CFPB has no jurisdiction here — but the EPA Office of Inspector General does: 1-888-372-7341. File a complaint about DEP's failure to enforce federal standards.
  • U.S. Representative's office: Congressional inquiry letters force agency responses on the record
⚖️ Step 3 — Legal Action Available to You
  • Clean Water Act § 1365 citizen suit: Any person may sue a CWA violator directly in federal court after 60-day notice. The Kopperston and Lynco notices are already filed — contact Appalachian Mountain Advocates to join or understand your rights.
  • Private well damage — common law tort: If a company's actions contaminated your private water supply, you have a civil damages claim for the cost of remediation, loss of property value, and personal injury
  • SMCRA petition: Any person may petition the state regulatory authority to inspect a mine operation for violations
  • Contact: Appalachian Mountain Advocates — appalmtnadvocates.org
🔬 Step 4 — Demand Independent Testing
  • Request testing through Appalachian Voices (appvoices.org) — they have the scientists and methodology that will hold up in court
  • Ask for testing of: pH, iron, manganese, aluminum, selenium, sulfate, bacteria (coliform/E.coli), methane dissolved in water
  • If the DEP offers to test — accept it, but also get your own independent test on the same day so you have a comparison
  • Keep a chain of custody for every sample — who collected it, when, where, and what happened to it
