📜 The Law That Protected You — 1935 to 2005

The Public Utility Holding Company Act of 1935 — What It Did and Why It Existed

After the Great Depression — caused in part by the collapse of massive, unregulated utility holding company pyramids — Congress passed the Public Utility Holding Company Act of 1935 (PUHCA). It was a New Deal consumer protection law.

PUHCA had many provisions. Most people know it as a law that broke up utility monopolies. But it had a second provision almost nobody talks about today — and it was the most powerful consumer protection in the law:

PUHCA Section — Political Contribution Ban:

From 1935 to 2005, PUHCA-regulated utilities were prohibited by federal law from using corporate treasury funds to contribute to local, state, and federal political parties and candidates.

For 70 years, the power company literally could not write a check to the politician who sets the rules the power company has to follow.

That protection existed for 70 years. It was repealed. You were never told.
Source: Utility Dive, December 19, 2023, citing Utility Dive contributor Mark Van Orden (CGO/Utah State University / UC Irvine). Original law: Public Utility Holding Company Act of 1935, P.L. 74-333.

Why This Protection Existed in the First Place

Congress in 1935 understood something specific: utilities are monopolies. You cannot choose a different power company. You cannot choose a different water utility. Because consumers have no competitive alternative, the only real protection they have is that the regulator is genuinely independent. If the utility can buy the politician who appoints or elects the regulator, that independence is gone — and the consumer has no recourse at all. That is why the Founders of the regulatory state specifically banned utility political donations. They understood the problem 90 years ago.

💥 The 2005 Repeal — How the Protection Was Erased

The Energy Policy Act of 2005 — The Bill That Changed Everything

In 2005, Congress passed the Energy Policy Act of 2005. Buried inside it was the full repeal of PUHCA.

When PUHCA was repealed, the political contribution ban disappeared with it. Jurisdiction over utility political donations returned to the states. At that moment:

  • 20 states already had laws prohibiting corporations from making political donations — so the repeal changed nothing for them.
  • 30 states — including South Carolina and Virginia — allowed corporate contributions. When PUHCA was repealed, utility companies in those states were immediately free to donate to the politicians who control their regulatory environment.
What Utilities Did Immediately After the Repeal:
Peer-reviewed economic research found that utilities in the 30 newly-free states nearly doubled their political donations after 2005. They did not spread donations evenly — they concentrated them in the years when rate cases were being decided. The donations were largest when a Public Utility Commission was authorizing a new rate of return. That is not coincidence. That is investment. Van Orden, CGO/Utah State University, published Utility Dive, December 19, 2023

The Return on Investment — Quantified

The same research quantified what utilities got back for their donations:

  • Utilities that could donate received authorized Rates of Return (ROE) that were 0.4 percentage points higher than utilities in states that still prohibited donations.
  • For the average utility, that 0.4% ROE increase translates to approximately $4 million in additional annual revenue.
  • Donate hundreds of thousands of dollars. Get millions back in approved profit margin. Every year. Forever.
Source: Van Orden, "Power Play: Political Contributions and Regulatory Capture in Electric Utility Rate Cases," Center for Growth and Opportunity, Utah State University. Published Utility Dive, December 19, 2023.

📈 The Stock Ownership Question

Why Can They Own Stock in Companies They Vote to Make More Profitable?

The federal STOCK Act (2012) addresses congressional stock trading — Members of Congress cannot trade on nonpublic information. But it does not ban them from owning stock in regulated industries, and it does not apply to state legislators at all.

At the state level, the answer is almost everywhere the same: disclosure, not prohibition. You have to tell the public you own the stock. You do not have to sell it. You do not have to recuse yourself from votes that increase its value, unless the vote uniquely benefits you in a way it does not benefit other shareholders — which is nearly impossible to prove when a vote increases the value of a publicly traded company for all shareholders equally.

1
Virginia legislator owns $50,000 in Dominion stock. Vote comes up to increase Dominion's authorized profit margin from 9.3% to 9.7%. Every Dominion shareholder in the world benefits. The legislator's benefit is not unique to them. Under Virginia law — no recusal required. Under Clean Virginia's analysis, 9 Virginia legislators voted exactly this way. Clean Virginia, April 28, 2022
2
South Carolina legislator owns $80,000 in Duke Energy stock. Duke Energy's market cap is $80 billion. $80,000 is 0.0001% of outstanding shares — far below the 5%-of-class disclosure threshold in SC Code §8-13-700. Under SC law: no disclosure required. No recusal required. No one even knows. SC Code §8-13-700; SC School Board Association ethics handout
3
Both legislators vote to elect the PSC/SCC commissioner who then approves the rate increase that boosts the stock. Perfectly legal in both states. Not even technically a conflict of interest under current law because the benefit is diffuse — shared with all shareholders — not unique. National Conference of State Legislatures voting recusal provisions survey
4
Congress has tried — and failed — to pass stock trading bans multiple times. In 2026, legislation was introduced to ban Congressional members, spouses, and dependent children from purchasing publicly traded stocks. It has not passed. House Administration Committee, January 2026
The Core Legal Theory That Enables This:
For a conflict of interest to require recusal, the law generally requires that the official's financial interest be personal and unique — different from the interest of the general public. When a vote benefits a publicly traded company and thereby benefits all shareholders equally, courts and ethics commissions in most states have held that no individual shareholder has a "unique" interest requiring recusal. The more shareholders there are, the less any one person's conflict is "unique." The utility industry specifically chose to be publicly traded partly because this legal structure protects their political allies from recusal requirements.
🚨 Ohio — When "Legal" Became Criminal

FirstEnergy — What Happens When Donations Become Bribes

Ohio's FirstEnergy case shows exactly where the legal version ends and the criminal version begins — and how thin that line actually is.

  • FirstEnergy spent years making legal political donations to Ohio legislators and regulators — the same thing Virginia and South Carolina utilities do.
  • Then they crossed the line: they criminally bribed state officials to install a specific industry-friendly lobbyist as the head of the Ohio Public Utilities Commission — the regulator overseeing their rates.
  • The bribery scheme totaled $60 million.
  • Separately, they funded a $1 billion nuclear plant bailout bill — which passed — adding costs to Ohio ratepayers.
  • FirstEnergy was criminally charged. Multiple officials were convicted.
The Uncomfortable Truth Ohio Revealed:
The legal version — donating to legislators, funding caucuses, hiring former governors as lobbyists — produces the same result as the criminal version, just more slowly and with plausible deniability. You do not have to bribe someone if you can donate to their campaign legally, fund their party's caucus, and hire their predecessor as your lobbyist. The outcome — a regulator who is friendly to your rate increases — is identical. The mechanism is the only difference. One gets you prosecuted. The other gets you a $322 million rate increase approved by the PSC your money helped populate.
Source: Utility Dive, August 3, 2023 (FirstEnergy criminal charges). Van Orden, Utility Dive December 2023 (legal vs. illegal mechanisms producing same regulatory outcomes).
✅ What Would Actually Fix This

The Solutions — Why They Keep Not Passing

The peer-reviewed economic research is clear on what works. Here is the ranked list — from most to least effective — with the documented political fate of each:

SolutionWhy It WorksCurrent Status
1. Ban utility political contributions entirely This is what PUHCA did. Research shows it directly lowered authorized rates of return — meaning lower bills. The Tillman Act of 1907 and 36 states once had this. It worked for 70 years. Virginia: bipartisan ban bill proposed 2023, 2025 — both failed. South Carolina: not seriously proposed. Federal: not introduced.
2. Let voters elect utility commissioners directly Research shows elected commissioners authorize lower rates than appointed/legislature-elected commissioners. Voters are consumers — they respond to lower bills. Legislators respond to donors. 20 states elect commissioners by popular vote. Virginia and South Carolina both use legislature election — the two worst-performing models for consumers.
3. Cap corporate political contributions Research shows utilities in states with unlimited contributions receive higher authorized ROEs than in states with caps. A cap reduces the influence without eliminating it. 16 of the 30 states permitting corporate contributions have no caps. Neither Virginia nor South Carolina has a utility-specific cap.
4. Ban legislators from owning stock in regulated utilities Removes the financial incentive legislators have to vote for utility-friendly policies. Congress has tried and failed. No state has passed this for utility stock specifically. Federal bill introduced 2026 — not passed. Virginia: not introduced. South Carolina: not introduced.
5. Require real-time public stock disclosure At minimum, voters should know when their legislators have financial interests in the companies whose rates they're setting. Current state laws have the loopholes documented in the SC/VA comparison page. Partial. VA requires disclosure at $5K dividends. SC's 5%-of-class threshold is functionally useless for large utilities.
"In states that relied on PUHCA to govern utilities' political donations, it is time to reevaluate whether their current laws protect the integrity and independence of their PUCs or leave room for undue influence in regulated industries."

The One Thing Individuals Can Do Right Now:
Both Virginia and South Carolina have pending utility rate cases before their regulators. You have the right to file formal public comments. You have the right to petition to intervene as a party. The tools exist — the system is designed so that almost nobody uses them, because the process is opaque and the utilities have hundreds of lawyers. But the window is open. Every formal comment on the record makes the case harder to quietly approve. Every intervenor costs the utility time and money. It is not nothing.
