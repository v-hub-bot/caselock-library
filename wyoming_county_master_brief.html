The Legal Case

Every Federal and State Law Being Violated — With Enforcement Mechanisms

⚖️ 1. Clean Water Act (CWA) — 33 U.S.C. § 1251 et seq. — THE STRONGEST TOOL
  • § 1311 — Prohibits all unpermitted discharges of pollutants into waters of the US. The Kopperston site is discharging without an NPDES permit — this is a per se violation.
  • § 1342 — NPDES permit requirements. Lexington Coal has a permit (WV0056693) and has violated it 186 times since Oct. 2021. Every violation is a separate $25,000–$50,000/day federal civil penalty exposure under § 1319.
  • § 1365 — CITIZEN SUIT PROVISION. Any person may sue any CWA violator directly in U.S. District Court after providing 60-day written notice. The notices for Kopperston (Rockwell Mining) and Lynco (Lexington Coal) were filed Aug. 29, 2025. Litigation window: opened Oct. 28, 2025. The lawsuit can be filed NOW.
  • § 1319 — Enforcement. Civil penalties up to $25,000/day per violation for the first violation, $37,500/day for continuing violations. Criminal penalties for willful or negligent violations.
  • WHO CAN FILE: Any citizen, any environmental organization, Mountain Water Watch itself, with an attorney. No need to be personally harmed — proximity and environmental interest is sufficient standing.
⚖️ 2. Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act (SMCRA) — 30 U.S.C. § 1201 et seq.
  • § 1265 — Requires coal mine operators to restore land and water to approximate original contour and pre-mining conditions. Every flooded mine in Wyoming County that has not been reclaimed is a potential SMCRA violation.
  • § 1268 — Civil penalties up to $5,000 per day per violation for failure to comply with a cease and desist order.
  • Citizen petition right: Any person may petition the state regulatory authority (WVDEP) to conduct an inspection for SMCRA violations. If WVDEP ignores the petition, the petitioner can file suit in federal court under § 1270.
  • Bankruptcy does NOT extinguish SMCRA obligations. Courts have held that reclamation duties transfer to acquiring companies — Bluestone Resources acquiring Pinnacle Mining assets = Bluestone inheriting cleanup obligations.
  • Alpha Natural Resources precedent (2014): Public Justice + Appalachian Mountain Advocates successfully forced Alpha to install conductivity treatment technology under the CWA. The Lynco and Kopperston cases follow the identical legal theory.
⚖️ 3. Endangered Species Act (ESA) — 16 U.S.C. § 1531 et seq.
  • § 9 — Prohibits any person from "taking" a listed species, including through habitat modification that kills or injures the species through impairment of essential behavioral patterns.
  • The Guyandotte River Crayfish is federally listed as endangered and the Guyandotte River is designated critical habitat. Both the Kopperston and Lynco discharges flow into tributaries of the Guyandotte.
  • Testing confirmed pH 4.7, selenium 40× the limit, aluminum 108× the limit at outfall points draining to the Guyandotte — "at these levels, there are negative impacts to the small streams where the discharge is going in directly" — Appalachian Voices scientist Matt Hepler, Aug. 2025.
  • § 11(g) — Citizen suit provision: Any person may sue any violator of § 9, or any federal agency for failure to carry out its ESA duties, after 60-day notice. No need to own property in the affected area.
  • Federal agency nexus: Any federal permit, license, or funding connected to operations harming critical habitat independently triggers ESA § 7 consultation requirements that could block or condition the activities.
⚖️ 4. Safe Drinking Water Act (SDWA) — 42 U.S.C. § 300f et seq.
  • The Guyandotte River is a source of the local public drinking water supply. Contamination of its tributaries is not just an ecological issue — it directly threatens public water infrastructure.
  • Wyoming County already ranks among the 10 worst counties in the nation for SDWA violations per peer-reviewed study published in Risk Analysis (2025).
  • Public water system users have complaint rights under SDWA — formal complaints to EPA Region 3 (r3.public@epa.gov) and WVDEP trigger mandatory response timelines.
  • Underground Injection Control (UIC) Program — § 300h: If wastewater injection (fracking-related or data center-related) is occurring near drinking water sources without proper permits, this is a direct SDWA violation reportable to EPA.
⚖️ 5. West Virginia Code — State Law Tools
  • § 22-11 Water Pollution Control Act: Prohibits any discharge causing water quality to violate state standards. WVDEP's own sampling showing violations creates a direct enforcement obligation.
  • § 22-2-10 Mine Drainage Treatment: Requires treatment of mine drainage to reduce toxic substances. The Legislature explicitly found that failure to treat mine drainage causes environmental harm — this is a statutory finding usable in court.
  • § 22-7-3 Surface Owner Compensation: Oil and gas developers must compensate surface owners for damaged water supplies, lost income, and expenses. This applies to any operator whose underground activities have damaged private wells.
  • § 46A — Consumer Credit and Protection Act: Deceptive practices causing economic harm have a private right of action. If any operator misrepresented impacts to residents, this may apply.
  • WV Constitution, Article VI § 35 — Right to a clean environment: West Virginia's constitution contains explicit environmental protection provisions that can be raised in state court actions.
⚖️ 6. Common Law — Civil Damages — Personal Injury & Property
  • Nuisance (private and public): Unreasonable interference with the use and enjoyment of private property. Contaminated well water is a classic private nuisance. Affects on a community = public nuisance actionable by the state AG.
  • Trespass to land: Physical invasion of property by pollutants from another's operation — even underground migration of contaminated water constitutes trespass in most jurisdictions.
  • Negligence: Failure to exercise reasonable care in mining/industrial operations that foreseeably causes harm to neighboring property owners.
  • Strict liability: Abnormally dangerous activities (underground blasting, high-pressure mine flooding) — some jurisdictions impose strict liability without requiring proof of negligence.
  • Damages available: Cost of new well or water system, cost of filtration, medical monitoring, personal injury, property value loss, emotional distress. Roy New's $11,000 well alone is documented economic harm.

🔬 USGS — Cone of Depression / Groundwater Pressure

Source: USGS Water Science School "Aquifers and Groundwater" · USGS Circular 1217 · USGS media image "Cone of depression: pumping a well can cause water level lowering"

  • When a large well pumps at high volume, it creates a cone-shaped depression in the water table around it — reversing the normal direction of groundwater flow
  • This pulls water AND contaminants from neighboring areas toward the powerful well and destabilizes pressure in surrounding aquifers
  • In a region already destabilized by flooded mines under pressure, high-volume pumping by any new industrial user (data center, gas well) can act as a trigger for sudden contamination events — exactly what Brenton residents described happening overnight
🔬 Illinois State Water Survey (ISWS) — Aquifer Depletion Science

Source: Prairie Research Institute, University of Illinois, 2015/2018. National Ground Water Association Outstanding Award 2019. AGU Water Resources Research doi:10.1029/2020WR028844.

  • Deep aquifer systems like those feeding into Appalachia are NOT recharged by rainfall — extraction is effectively permanent
  • Applies directly to the Brenton situation: the mine flooding is not filling the aquifer with clean water — it is contaminating the only water available
  • Even a 35% reduction in withdrawals still leaves aquifer areas at High or Severe risk — the scale of data center demand makes aquifer recovery impossible if both threats are allowed simultaneously
🔬 Risk Analysis Journal — Wyoming County SDWA Violations

Source: Risk Analysis (peer-reviewed journal), 2025 study. Cited by West Virginia Rivers Coalition and Facebook posts from wvrivers, 2025.

  • Wyoming County is ranked #1 in the entire United States for Safe Drinking Water Act violations
  • Two Wyoming County public water systems had the highest number of drinking-water violations recorded for any single system during the study period
  • This is a national ranking of government-confirmed regulatory failures — not community complaint, but official violation records analyzed by academic researchers
🔬 EPA Conductivity Science — Appalachian Streams

Source: EPA peer-reviewed report 2011; confirmed in OVEC v. Elk Run Coal Company (2014).

  • EPA determined that most streams downstream from mines with valley fills are biologically impaired
  • High conductivity (dissolved salt contamination from mine drainage) causes 10–20× increases in electrical conductivity, eliminating aquatic life
  • EPA's benchmark for safe conductivity levels has been established and peer-reviewed — courts have accepted this science in citizen suit cases
It Can Be Won — Precedents

Cases That Were Exactly Like This — And Were Won

✅ OVEC v. Elk Run Coal Company (2014) — Alpha Natural Resources — WV
  • What happened: Citizen groups sued Alpha Natural Resources for CWA violations causing biological impairment of WV streams through high conductivity pollution from valley fills
  • Who won: The citizens. U.S. District Judge found Alpha liable in June 2014
  • Result: Settlement requiring Alpha to install state-of-the-art conductivity treatment technology — first legal precedent of its kind in Appalachia
  • Who did it: Public Justice + Appalachian Mountain Advocates + West Virginia Sierra Club + Ohio Valley Environmental Coalition + WV Highlands Conservancy
  • Why it applies: Exact same legal theory — CWA citizen suit, mine drainage contamination, biological impairment of WV streams. The precedent is set. Lynco and Kopperston follow this directly.
✅ Federal Court Orders WV to Clean Abandoned Mine Drainage — Public Justice Win
  • What happened: Public Justice won a federal court order requiring West Virginia to clean up toxic acid mine drainage from abandoned coal mines so discharges comply with water quality standards
  • Why it applies: Establishes that abandoned mine operators — or states that take over abandoned mine operations — cannot simply ignore water quality standards because the mine is "inactive"
  • Contact: publicjustice.net — Environmental Enforcement Project
✅ Saline Township, Michigan — Data Center Water Lawsuit (2024)
  • What happened: Residents sued a data center operator in Michigan over groundwater depletion and contamination linked to industrial cooling water use
  • Result: Case established standing for residential plaintiffs to challenge data center water permits and impacts
  • Why it applies: The cone-of-depression mechanism is now documented in court — data centers pulling groundwater destabilize neighboring wells. WV residents facing the same threat have this precedent.
✅ Earthjustice + NAACP v. xAI — Clean Air Act (April 2026)
  • What happened: Earthjustice, SELC, and NAACP sued xAI for operating 27 unpermitted gas turbines powering its data center in Southaven, Mississippi under the Clean Air Act
  • Legal theory: Operating a major emission source without a Title V air permit is a federal violation regardless of state approval
  • Why it applies: Any power generation infrastructure being built to serve WV data centers without proper air permits faces the same Clean Air Act exposure. This precedent is active and brand-new.
  • Contact: Earthjustice — earthjustice.org
✅ WV Highlands Conservancy + Appalachian Voices v. South Fork Coal — 2025
  • What happened: WV Highlands Conservancy and Appalachian Voices sued South Fork Coal Company for ongoing CWA violations in WV
  • Status: Active 2025 litigation — establishes current standing and active litigation environment in WV courts
✅ Sierra Club v. WVDEP — Clean Water Act Mountaintop Removal Permit Blocked (April 2026)
  • What happened: A U.S. District Judge in the Southern District of West Virginia blocked a Clean Water Act permit for a proposed mountaintop removal mine, siding with Coal River Mountain Watch and the Sierra Club
  • Why it applies: April 2026 ruling — the Southern District of West Virginia is actively ruling against WVDEP and coal operators on CWA grounds. This is the same court where the Kopperston/Lynco suits would be filed.

The Coming Double Threat

Why Data Centers Make the Coal Mine Problem Unsolvable Without Legal Action Now

🚨 WV HB 2014 + HB 4983 — The Law That Stripped Local Protection — Feb 17, 2026

West Virginia's legislature passed rules stripping every local government of the authority to enforce ANY ordinance against certified data center developments — no noise limits, no water use limits, no pollution controls. Water transparency amendment killed 23–70.

This means: If a data center comes to Wyoming County, the County Commission cannot stop it. Cannot limit its water use. Cannot require disclosure of its impact on wells. Cannot enforce any local ordinance against it. The only tools remaining are federal law and state court.

The Compound Mechanism — Why Both Threats Together Are Catastrophic
  1. Step 1 — Baseline: Pinnacle Mining Complex is flooded. Underground pressure has been forcing contaminated mine water up through the ground into private wells since at least 2020. Brenton, Indian Creek, Wolf Pen, Baileysville — all affected. DEP dismissed its own lawsuit. Bluestone/Alpha/Rockwell are still in business.
  2. Step 2 — Rollback: Trump EPA finalized MATS rule repeal Feb. 19, 2026. More mercury, arsenic, and heavy metals are now legally permitted in air and water discharges from coal operations. The already-contaminated watershed gets worse on paper.
  3. Step 3 — Data Center arrival: WV is actively recruiting data centers to the coalfields region. A single large data center draws up to 5 million gallons/day from groundwater. This creates a massive cone of depression (USGS) — reversing groundwater flow, destabilizing aquifer pressure, and pulling contamination from the flooded mine systems toward new extraction points.
  4. Step 4 — HB 2014: Local government cannot stop it, limit it, or require it to disclose its water impact. The only protection is federal law — which the Trump administration is simultaneously rolling back.
  5. Step 5 — The overnight event: Roy New's well went from clean to yellow-brown overnight. Bubbling. Gas smell. Multiple households simultaneously. This is a pressure event — consistent with a flooded mine system disturbed by a new large-volume extraction nearby. This is what the cone of depression does.
🔬 What Data Centers Actually Put Into Water — Verified Chemistry
  • Cooling tower blowdown: Contains biocides (to kill algae), anti-corrosion agents, hexavalent chromium, scale inhibitors — all industrial chemicals capable of groundwater contamination if discharge is improperly managed (DataCenterKnowledge 2024; FWPCOA 2024)
  • Backup diesel storage: Data centers carry thousands of gallons of diesel on-site for generators. Spills and leaks reach soil and aquifer (Portland Press Herald 2024)
  • PFAS plumes: Erin Brockovich's April 28, 2026 report states data centers are 'already being flagged for emitting PFAS plumes' — this is her documented concern based on fire suppression systems and cooling chemicals. Academic literature (Innovation News Network 2024; International Fire & Safety Journal) also flags the PFAS risk from data center fire suppression systems.
  • 80–90% of cooling water permanently lost to evaporation — it does not return to the aquifer (EESI 2024)
