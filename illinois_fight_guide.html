WHAT IS A "CONE OF DEPRESSION" AND WHY YOUR NEIGHBORS' WELLS MATTER

When a large facility pumps massive volumes of water from a local aquifer, it creates what scientists call a "cone of depression" — a funnel-shaped drop in the surrounding water table. Private wells within that cone can go dry within weeks or months. Municipal wells drawing from the same aquifer see reduced pressure and output.

⚠️ FOR SMALL ILLINOIS TOWNS: If your town is on a shallow drift aquifer (most rural towns are), a data center drawing even 500,000 gallons per day can pull the water table below the intake level of residential wells within a 1–2 mile radius. Once those wells go dry, you're on bottled water or you're paying to drill deeper — at your own expense.
THE "THEY'LL RETURN THE WATER" MYTH — DEBUNKED
Common claim from data center developers: "We return wastewater to the system."

The truth: Cooling towers work by evaporation. The water is intentionally turned into vapor to carry heat away from the servers. That vapor rises into the atmosphere. It does not fall back into your local aquifer — it joins the regional weather system and may fall as rain hundreds of miles away. The "return" is to a distant watershed, not yours.

FINDING 2 — SINKHOLES: KARST AQUIFER COLLAPSE — ILLINOIS-SPECIFIC RISK

Sinkholes form when groundwater is withdrawn from karst limestone aquifers — exactly the geology underlying much of Illinois, including the Cambrian-Ordovician aquifer system. When water is removed faster than it recharges, the rock structure loses its support and collapses. A York County, Pennsylvania community meeting specifically raised sinkhole risk from data center water extraction from limestone geology. The Lockport/Will County region sits above the same karst limestone formation. A single hyperscale data center can extract 365+ million gallons per year — enough to destabilize the water table in communities already drawing from a depleted aquifer.

Sources: Tandfonline karst/sinkhole peer-reviewed study 2021 · York County PA community meeting record · Great Lakes Report Aug 2025 · Joyce Foundation groundwater report

❓ HONEST QUESTION — VERIFIED ANSWER

"How does a data center actually turn the water brown?" — Three mechanisms, all documented

1. Water Table Drop → Sediment Intrusion. Data centers extract millions of gallons daily. When the water table drops rapidly, wells that previously drew clean water from mid-aquifer depths now pull from lower sediment-heavy layers — or pull in surrounding soil through newly-created pressure differentials. The brown color is iron, manganese, and clay sediment that was previously separated from the drinking water zone. This is the most likely mechanism in Morgan County, GA.

2. Construction Disturbance → Runoff Into Aquifer. Heavy construction (grading, foundation work, blasting) disturbs the soil seal between surface contamination and groundwater. Chemical runoff, diesel, and construction chemicals can infiltrate the aquifer directly. Ben Cole's comment noting it "looks like condensate water corrosive enough to eat concrete" is consistent with construction chemical contamination.

3. Thermal Pollution → Bacterial Growth. Data centers discharge warm water. Elevated water temperatures suppress natural bacterial inhibitors and promote iron bacteria and manganese bacteria growth — both produce brown/rust-colored water and are documented health concerns. Carrie Ewell's comment that data centers "raise water temperature by at least 3 degrees" is consistent with documented thermal discharge data.

Answer verified against: USGS groundwater mechanics · Lincoln Institute land/water impacts report · EPA thermal discharge standards · Joyce Foundation data center groundwater report
