📍 Where Fluvanna Fits

One Small County — Four Industrial Fights Simultaneously

Fluvanna County sits in Virginia's Piedmont region, bordered by the James River and bisected by the Rivanna River — Virginia's first designated state Scenic River. Population approximately 27,000. County seat: Palmyra.

Starting in 2024 and accelerating through 2026, Fluvanna found itself at the intersection of four separate industrial development pressures that together represent a complete transformation of the county's rural character — all happening at the same time, with limited public disclosure.

The Core Injustice, Stated Simply: Rural Fluvanna County is being asked to host the largest gas generation site in Virginia, a high-voltage transmission superhighway, secret data center deals, and an Amazon warehouse — all to power and serve Northern Virginia's tech industry. 🖥️ Fight #1 — Data Centers

How Fluvanna Became One of the Few Counties to Actually Win This Round

September 17, 2025
Board of Supervisors approves an immediate moratorium on all new data center applications. Effective immediately, expires January 31, 2026. Fluvanna County BOS meeting record; Fluvanna County official website
November 19, 2025
Planning Commission votes 5–0 to strip data centers of by-right status in ALL I-1 and I-2 industrial zones. Any future project now requires a Special Use Permit and full public review. Residents packed the room and overwhelmingly supported the change. Fluvanna Review, November 26, 2025
January 2026
Board of Supervisors formally adopts the change — eliminating by-right data center zoning countywide. This is a permanent policy shift, not a moratorium. Fluvanna Review, January 1, 2026 year-in-review
January 31, 2026
Moratorium expires — but the permanent Special Use Permit requirement remains. New applications can be filed, but now face full public scrutiny. Fluvanna County moratorium documents
What This Means: Before this change, a developer could build a data center in any industrial zone in Fluvanna without a public hearing. Now every data center proposal triggers a formal public review process with community input. This is the same protection that Prince William, Loudoun, and Fluvanna fought for — and Fluvanna actually got it.

What Residents Said at the November 2025 Hearing

Speakers at the public hearing cited the following concerns — on the record:

  • Noise from large-scale cooling systems
  • Water consumption competing with residential needs
  • Electric grid strain driving up utility rates for residents
  • Minimal actual job creation relative to industrial footprint
  • Threat to the county's rural character and property values
Source: Fluvanna Review, November 26, 2025 — direct coverage of the public hearing.
⚠️ The NDA Complication: According to the Fluvanna Review's 2025 year-in-review (January 1, 2026), supervisors signed restrictive non-disclosure agreements that legally prevented them from discussing certain development projects — including the Amazon Rt. 15 deal — with their own constituents. The Virginia Mercury documented this practice statewide: "Data centers, non-disclosure agreements and democracy" (April 30, 2025). Residents interpreted supervisor silence as betrayal. For some, it was legally enforced silence.
Sources: Fluvanna Review year-in-review, January 1, 2026; Virginia Mercury, April 30, 2025.
🔥 Fight #2 — The Tenaska Gas Plant

The Biggest Loss — A Midnight Vote That Overrode the Community

Nebraska-based Tenaska has operated a 940-megawatt methane gas plant in Fluvanna since 2004 — built despite strong community opposition at the time. In August 2025, they announced plans for a second, even larger 1,540-megawatt plant on the same Branch Road site. The project would make Fluvanna home to the largest site of gas generation in all of Virginia.

This is the same pattern seen statewide: community bodies make evidence-based decisions, elected officials override them under economic pressure.

Why Is This Plant Being Built? — The Data Center Connection

Virginia's electricity demand is projected to triple over the next 15 years — driven almost entirely by data center growth, primarily in Northern Virginia. Tenaska's new plant is explicitly designed to feed that demand through the PJM regional grid. Fluvanna County bears the pollution, noise, and water withdrawal. Northern Virginia's data centers get the power.

Source: JLARC (Virginia's General Assembly research arm) 2024 data center electricity demand analysis; Fluvanna Review; Appalachian Voices.
📦 Fight #3 — The Amazon Warehouse & The NDA Problem

Secret Deals, Silenced Supervisors, and a Warehouse Nobody Voted For

In late 2025, residents in the Zion Crossroads area of Fluvanna noticed trees being cleared along U.S. Route 15. The county had not publicly announced any development. Social media posts began circulating in community groups: "It's NOT WAWA it's AMAZON."

When Palmyra Supervisor Tim Hodge held a town hall on December 4, 2025, residents pressed him for answers. He confirmed that Amazon is pursuing construction of a warehouse on Rt. 15 — likely to operate as a local delivery hub. He described it as a delivery facility, not a full-scale distribution center.

The NDA Problem — Documented Statewide:
The Fluvanna Review's January 1, 2026 year-in-review specifically documented that "citizens have also raised concerns about supervisors signing restrictive non-disclosure agreements that have often prevented them from speaking with constituents about projects such as Tenaska and a new Amazon distribution center on Rt. 15."

The Virginia Mercury published a direct investigation into this practice on April 30, 2025: "Data centers, non-disclosure agreements and democracy" — documenting how Big Tech companies routinely require local officials to sign NDAs before deal negotiations, legally preventing elected representatives from informing the people who elected them.

In Louisiana, officials signed NDAs as Amazon's $12 billion data center unfolded in secret. One Louisiana official called it "a muzzle on elected officials." (WWNO, March 2026.)

Resident Concerns at the December 2025 Town Hall

  • "What is that Amazon warehouse going to do to the land value residential around that warehouse?"
  • Concerns about truck traffic on residential roads near Rt. 15
  • Concerns that industrial growth in Zion Crossroads was outpacing the county's infrastructure capacity
  • A general "growing sense of distrust in county leadership" — documented by the Fluvanna Review
Sources: Fluvanna Review, December 10, 2025 (Hodge town hall coverage); Fluvanna Review, January 1, 2026 (year-in-review); Virginia Mercury, April 30, 2025; WWNO, March 27, 2026.

Valley Link is proposing a 115-mile, 765-kilovolt transmission line running from Campbell County to Culpeper County — cutting through nine Virginia localities including Fluvanna. It is one of the highest-capacity transmission systems in the United States.

The Critical Limitation — On the Record: Fluvanna Supervisor Mike Goad stated directly: "I do wish we had more of a formal say in the matter." Ultimate authority over the Valley Link project rests with the Virginia SCC — not with Fluvanna County. The county can oppose it. They cannot stop it alone. Why Is This Line Being Built?

Dominion Energy representatives told the Board that without additional transmission infrastructure, existing lines in central and northern Virginia "could face overload conditions as early as 2029." Electricity demand could double by 2035 — driven largely by data centers. The Valley Link line is designed to move power generated in the Ohio River Valley east to Northern Virginia's data center corridor. Rural counties like Fluvanna provide the right-of-way corridor. Northern Virginia gets the power.

"Something that was said earlier was that they're interested in maintaining the electric grid integrity. I'm interested in maintaining the integrity of Fluvanna County."
— Amy Williams, Fluvanna resident, at the April 1, 2026 Board meeting. Quoted in Fluvanna Review, April 9, 2026.
💧 Fight #5 — Aqua Virginia at Lake Monticello

4,600 Households — Water Quality Problems & A 29% Rate Hike Request

Aqua Virginia serves the Lake Monticello community in Fluvanna County — approximately 4,600 households. In 2024, Aqua filed for a 29.49% rate increase before the SCC (Case PUR-2023-00073). The Lake Monticello Owners Association (LMOA) formally intervened and produced a 27-page rebuttal report — one of the most detailed community responses to a utility rate case in Virginia's recent history.

What LMOA Found in Aqua's Own Data

  • 580 community complaints filed with the SCC by Lake Monticello alone — covering odor, water quality, low pressure, and wastewater spills into the Lake
  • Aqua's air quality study after years of odor complaints was conducted over just 10 days around Thanksgiving — and excluded the wastewater treatment plant, which residents identified as the primary odor source
  • Aqua sent a letter claiming LMOA was in violation for "allowing boats to dump sewer tanks at the Marina" — a marina that has no toilets, no dumping site, and isn't even connected to Aqua's wastewater system
  • Aqua cut service to a senior community pool and clubhouse "after discovering it had never installed a water meter" — the facility had been connected to Aqua's system for 15 years
  • Aqua chose an "inflation peak" test year (April 2022–March 2023) to justify rate increases, even as inflation has since declined
The Cross-Subsidy Problem — Documented: LMOA's report found that Aqua Virginia owns 191 water systems and 9 sewage treatment systems statewide. Most are too small to be financially self-sustaining. Aqua uses larger systems like Lake Monticello to fund operations at the smaller, underperforming ones — meaning Lake Monticello customers are paying rates higher than necessary to subsidize Aqua's failing infrastructure elsewhere. LMOA called this "unjustly discriminatory."
"If Aqua Virginia's requested increase is approved, Lake Monticello customers whose fixed income is limited to Social Security retirement benefits will need 65 percent of their increase for 2024 just to pay for the increase in their 3,000-gallon bill from Aqua Virginia."
— LMOA Community Development Committee, 27-page formal SCC rebuttal report, February 2024. Reported by Fluvanna Review, February 8, 2024.

The current pending rate case — SCC Case PUR-2025-00071 — involves a 33.88% rate increase request covering Aqua's Virginia service area broadly, including Lake Monticello and Caroline County communities.

Source: SCC public docket PUR-2025-00071; Fluvanna Review, October 2025.

A new grassroots coalition formed in 2025, specifically to fight the Tenaska expansion.
