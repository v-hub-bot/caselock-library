Section 1 — Your Water Sources & Why They Are Vulnerable Right Now

Bowling Green Depends on ONE Water Source. A Professional Hydrologist Already Warned the City.

The Maumee River — Sole Water Source for Bowling Green

Bowling Green draws its entire drinking water supply from the Maumee River. The city's Water Treatment Plant stores raw water in a reservoir providing approximately 30 days of supply. The city treats 5–8 million gallons per day under normal conditions.

🔴 VERIFIED PROOF — On the Record Warning to City Council, November 21, 2025

Professional geologist and hydrologist Jim Evans — a Bowling Green resident — formally warned Bowling Green City Council that sole reliance on the Maumee River is a serious and growing vulnerability. His exact findings, presented to council on November 18, 2025:

  • Northwestern Ohio has experienced extreme drought conditions 7 times since 2000 — including the last two consecutive years
  • 2025 drought: 7-inch rainfall deficit directly centered over the Maumee River Valley
  • Mean annual temperatures in NW Ohio have risen 2.5°F (1.5°C) since 1950
  • Heavy rains are 24% more frequent but do not recharge groundwater — most water is lost as runoff
  • The Maumee River channel was approximately 50% dry by end of October 2025
  • Evans specifically cited concern about the city's 1 million gallon per day water commitment to the Meta data center as a threat during drought conditions

Evans' direct quote about the data center: "I understand the need for the city to pursue economic development projects, but I would caution that those kinds of commitments should have some restrictions attached."

He also stated: "The goal here should not be to make water distribution commitments to the maximum extent of our ability to pump water from the Maumee River during a good year, because difficult years are sure to come."

The Regional Carbonate Aquifer — Wood County's Hidden Vulnerability

Wood County's hydrogeologic system consists of a regional carbonate (limestone) aquifer buried beneath deposits of glacial till and lacustrine deposits. This is a karst-type formation. The Ohio ODNR Geological Survey has published a Groundwater Vulnerability Map for Wood County specifically rating areas for susceptibility to contamination based on soil, geology, and topography.

  • Carbonate/karst aquifers are highly vulnerable to contamination — pollutants travel rapidly through fractures and dissolution channels with minimal natural filtration
  • The same geology that makes Illinois vulnerable to sinkholes applies directly to Wood County
  • Construction of the Apollo gas plant involves two 16-inch natural gas pipelines — any subsurface leak enters the carbonate aquifer directly
  • 54 private water wells within one mile of the site draw from this same aquifer system
  • The ODNR's own assessment said impacts are "not expected" — but the assessment was based on normal conditions, not the drought-stressed, high-withdrawal scenario now created by the Meta commitment

Section 2 — Ohio Bill of Rights & Constitutional Weapons

The Ohio Constitution Gives Residents Specific Rights. These Have Been Used in Court.

Ohio Constitution — Article I, Section 1 — Inalienable Rights (THE BIG ONE)

"All men are, by nature, free and independent, and have certain inalienable rights, among which are those of enjoying and defending life and liberty, acquiring, possessing, and protecting property, and seeking and obtaining happiness and safety."

How to use it: The Ohio Supreme Court ruled in City of Norwood v. Horney (2006) that Article I, Section 1 creates an independently enforceable inalienable right to protect property — stronger than federal takings protections. A 350MW gas plant and a hyperscale data center that degrade air quality, water quality, and property values near residential neighborhoods directly implicate this right. Residents can argue the state's approval process — which bypassed local oversight — violated their inalienable right to protect their property and safety.

✅ CASE LAW PROOF — City of Norwood v. Horney, 110 Ohio St.3d 353 (2006) ✅ OHIO SUPREME COURT — BINDING

The Ohio Supreme Court held that Article I, Section 1's inalienable rights — including property protection — are independently enforceable under the Ohio Constitution, providing stronger protections than the federal Fourteenth Amendment. The court specifically noted that "the acquisition, possession, and protection of property" is inalienable. Applied here: residents have a constitutional right to protection of their property from industrial degradation that the state approved without their meaningful participation.

Source: City of Norwood v. Horney, 110 Ohio St.3d 353, 2006-Ohio-3799 · Ohio Constitution Article I, Section 1 · codes.ohio.gov/ohio-constitution/article-1
Ohio Constitution — Article I, Section 16 — Right to Remedy

"All courts shall be open, and every person, for an injury done him in his land, goods, person, or reputation, shall have remedy by due course of law."

How to use it: If the Meta data center or Apollo gas plant damages a resident's water well, property value, or health — Ohio's constitution guarantees access to court for remedy. This provision has been used to challenge state laws that limit a citizen's ability to sue for environmental damage. The ORC 4906.13(B) preemption clause — which strips local oversight — can be challenged as violating this provision by removing residents' practical ability to seek remedy before harm occurs.

Ohio Constitution — Article I, Section 19 — Private Property Protection

"Private property shall ever be held inviolate, but subservient to the public welfare. Where private property shall be taken for public welfare or for private use, the necessity for such taking shall first be judicially ascertained."

How to use it: A private corporation (Meta) is using state authority (OPSB preemption) to override a community's property rights for purely private commercial benefit. This is a taking argument — the state is using its power to benefit a private corporation at the expense of 54 well owners and nearby property owners. Norwood v. Horney specifically rejected "economic development" as a valid public welfare justification for overriding private property rights.

Ohio Constitution — Due Process — Procedural Challenge

The Apollo gas plant was approved with zero public hearings. Fourteen people submitted comments — all opposed — all ignored. The facility was auto-approved in 90 days. Ohio courts have held that meaningful notice and opportunity to be heard are procedural due process requirements before major state decisions affecting private property and health are finalized.

The Hilliard precedent directly applies: Hilliard's ERAC appeal argues the Ohio EPA failed to give proper statutory notice to local government before issuing the air permit. Norwich Township never received the required notice. The same statutory notice requirement under ORC 4906 applies to every affected locality. Check whether Middleton Township and Wood County received the legally required notifications — if not, the approval is procedurally defective.

⚖️ HOW THESE PRECEDENTS WORK IN OHIO COURT — IMPORTANT

Ohio cases (marked OH) — binding or directly applicable Ohio authority. An Ohio judge must take these seriously as precedent within the state's legal framework.

Federal cases (marked FED) — binding on all courts nationwide. Clean Air Act, SDWA, NEPA arguments apply in Ohio exactly as they apply everywhere else.

Out-of-state cases (marked OOS) — persuasive authority. An Ohio judge is not required to follow them, but any competent attorney can cite them to demonstrate that other courts, facing the same fact pattern and the same legal arguments, ruled in the community's favor. Courts regularly consider persuasive authority — especially when there is no controlling Ohio case on the specific issue yet.

The bottom line: Every case below is usable in Ohio. The distinction only affects how a judge weighs it — not whether it can be cited. A lawyer presenting the Prince William ruling to an Ohio judge introduces it as: "While not binding on this Court, we respectfully submit that the Virginia Court of Appeals' ruling in [case] is persuasive authority demonstrating that failure to provide adequate public notice voids a siting approval — a principle rooted in due process that applies in every jurisdiction."

Section 4 — Federal Laws That Apply Right Now

State Approval Does Not Override Federal Law. These Are Your Federal Hooks.

Clean Air Act — 42 U.S.C. § 7401 et seq. — Title V Major Source Permit

The Apollo gas plant is classified as a major emission source under the Ohio EPA Title V program — capable of 1.45 million pounds of CO₂ per day (equivalent to 66,000 cars, per Hilliard's filing). Title V permits are subject to EPA federal oversight and public challenge. Any person may petition the EPA Administrator to object to a state-issued Title V permit within 60 days. If the EPA fails to act, the person may seek judicial review in federal court. The xAI / Earthjustice lawsuit is using this exact provision.

Clean Water Act — 33 U.S.C. § 1251 — NPDES Permit Challenge

Any discharge of wastewater from the Apollo gas plant or Meta data center into waters of the United States (including Maumee River tributaries, drainage ditches connecting to navigable waters) requires an NPDES permit. Ohio EPA proposed a new general NPDES permit specifically for data center wastewater discharge — the Alliance for the Great Lakes and Great Lakes Environmental Law Center formally opposed it, citing hot, contaminated discharge threatening Ohio waterways. Any person may submit comments on open NPDES permits. Monitor the Ohio EPA permit database for any discharge permits associated with the Wood County site.

Safe Drinking Water Act — 42 U.S.C. § 300f — Well Water Protection

If any of the 54 private drinking water wells within one mile of the site are degraded by construction or operation, the Safe Drinking Water Act creates a federal cause of action. The EPA is required to protect underground sources of drinking water. Document every well — before and after each construction milestone. The ODNR's "not expected to impact" finding is a prediction, not a legal guarantee. It can be challenged in federal court if wells degrade.

Great Lakes – St. Lawrence River Basin Water Resources Compact — ORC Chapter 1522

Ohio is a compact signatory. Any water withdrawal exceeding 100,000 gallons per day from the Basin requires a permit and a finding of no significant adverse impact. A hyperscale data center drawing millions of gallons per day from the Maumee River — which drains to Lake Erie — must comply with Compact review requirements. Challenge: was a Compact review conducted for Meta's 1 million gallon/day commitment? If not, the water contract is potentially invalid under federal compact law.

National Environmental Policy Act — Federal Nexus Trigger

If ANY federal permit, federal funding, federal land, or FERC grid interconnection approval was involved in this project, NEPA review is mandatory. This includes: FERC grid interconnection for the Apollo plant (even behind-the-meter plants may have federal interconnection components), any USDA Rural Development involvement, any Army Corps of Engineers Section 404 permits for construction near Maumee River tributaries. File FOIA requests with FERC, Army Corps, USDA, and EPA to identify any federal nexus.

Section 5 — The Power Source Argument

The Apollo Gas Plant Is the Achilles Heel. Here Is Why.

Why the Gas Plant Is More Vulnerable Than the Data Center

The Meta data center is an approved commercial building. Challenging it directly is difficult. But the Apollo gas plant is a separate facility — separately permitted, separately approved, with its own Title V air permit still pending final issuance. It is the power source without which the data center cannot operate at full scale. Kill the power plant and you fundamentally change the data center's economics.

  • The plant is "behind the meter" — meaning it does not connect to the public grid. Without it, Meta must draw from the grid — creating a grid capacity problem that changes the entire project's regulatory profile
  • The Title V permit has not been finally issued. The 30-day ERAC appeal window opens when it is. Watch the Ohio EPA permit database.
  • The plant produces emissions equivalent to 66,000 cars per day — in a county with existing air quality issues. This is a Clean Air Act case.
  • Two 16-inch natural gas pipelines run underground to feed the plant — each requires separate siting and right-of-way approvals that can be independently challenged
