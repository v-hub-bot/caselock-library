⚖️ The SCC — Who Appoints the Regulators

How Virginia's Utility Regulator Is Chosen

The Virginia State Corporation Commission (SCC) has three commissioners who regulate all public utilities operating in Virginia — including Aqua Virginia. They set rates, approve mergers, and oversee service standards. Here is exactly how they are selected, per official sources:

1
SCC commissioners are elected by the Virginia General Assembly — not by Virginia voters — for six-year staggered terms. Ballotpedia — Virginia State Corporation Commission; Virginia SCC official website (scc.virginia.gov)
2
Current commissioners: Kelsey A. Bagot (Chair), elected by the General Assembly in 2024. Jehmal T. Hudson. Samuel T. Towell. Virginia SCC, scc.virginia.gov/about-the-scc/about-the-commissioners; Ballotpedia
3
The same Virginia legislators who vote to elect SCC commissioners also vote on legislation that directly affects utility profits and rates. Virginia Consumer Network (vcnva.org); Virginia Conservation Network (vcnva.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/08)
The Documented Conflict:

• Virginia's regulated electric utility monopolies have donated nearly $40 million to Virginia legislators since 1996 — the same legislators who vote to elect SCC commissioners. Virginia Consumer Network (vcnva.org/agenda-item/curbing-electric-utilities-political-influence-2024)

• Nine Virginia legislators were documented as owning stock in Dominion Energy while voting on legislation that increased the company's profits. None recused themselves. Clean Virginia (cleanvirginia.org, April 28, 2022, documented post)

• Virginia law requires disclosure when dividends exceed $5,000 — but does not require recusal from votes that increase a utility's value. Virginia Conflict of Interests Act; Virginia Consumer Network analysis

• Two bipartisan Virginia lawmakers — a Democratic senator and a Republican delegate — proposed bills to ban utilities from making political contributions to legislators. The bills did not pass. WRIC News; Clean Virginia (cleanvirginia.org); Virginia HB 2607, 2025 Regular Session (lis.virginia.gov)
Note: The $40 million figure covers all regulated electric utility monopolies — not Aqua/Essential Utilities specifically. Aqua's specific Virginia legislative contributions are documented separately via their PAC (see below).

⚖️ For Legal Counsel — The Systemic Argument

Why This Is Bigger Than a Rate Case

The following is a summary of the documented systemic pattern — presented as a framework for legal analysis, not as a legal conclusion. Each element below is sourced.

1
CEO of parent company paid $9.2 million in the same year the company filed for its 8th rate increase since 2006 — all approved. SEC filings; SCC docket history
2
Regulators who approve those rates are elected by legislators who receive documented political contributions from the utility industry. Ballotpedia; Virginia Consumer Network; OpenSecrets FEC C00340455
3
A bipartisan bill to ban these contributions failed to pass. Nine legislators voted it down — nine of whom had received Dominion contributions. WRIC News; lis.virginia.gov HB2607; VPAP data cited by WRIC
4
SCC complaint records dropped from 38 to 3 for the Lake Land'Or/Caroline area — a discrepancy documented by community advocates and flagged to the SCC. Community tracking; SCC staff testimony record; named resident Rachel Hart Rawlings
5
VDH confirmed elevated iron and manganese in the water supply — a state agency finding, not a resident allegation. Virginia Department of Health; cited in SCC case record
6
The pending merger requires SCC approval — creating a formal intervention window that bypasses the rate case process and opens full discovery. Virginia Code § 12.1-30; merger regulatory timeline
The Legal Frame — VCPA + Civil Rights: Virginia Consumer Protection Act § 59.1-200 prohibits "fraudulent, deceptive, or misleading" representations in consumer transactions. The pattern of filing for rate increases while suppressing complaint documentation, while regulators are appointed through a donation-funded legislative process, creates potential grounds for VCPA claims, EPA Safe Drinking Water Act enforcement, and federal civil rights claims under 42 U.S.C. § 1983 (deprivation of rights under color of state authority). These are frameworks for counsel to evaluate — not conclusions.
