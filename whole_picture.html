🔄 The National Pattern — Same Script, Every State

It Is the Same Playbook in Every Community. Once You See It, You Cannot Unsee It.

StageWhat HappensWhat It Looks Like Locally
1. Site Selection Company identifies rural/lower-income area with cheap land, cheap power, available water, and local officials who "need a boost to their economies" (Richard Kuser, May 2026 — Digital Realty community account) A few acres change hands quietly. Anonymous LLCs appear in county planning records. NDAs prevent officials from disclosing who is buying.
2. Economic Promise Company promises massive tax revenue and job creation. Numbers are presented without context — actual permanent jobs: 30-50 per facility. Tax revenue takes 5-10 years to materialize. Local officials vote 6-0 to approve. Community learns about it from a planning notice. "Jobs" are cited repeatedly. Nobody mentions water.
3. Infrastructure Demand Data center requires power and water commitments that exceed what local infrastructure can supply. Utilities are asked to build new lines, substations, and water agreements — costs get spread to all ratepayers. Dominion contracts 47 gigawatts of data center load — triples its peak capacity — without a single public vote on who pays for the grid upgrades.
4. Permit-by-Permit Approval Each project is reviewed individually. No cumulative impact assessment. By the time the 8th project is approved, the watershed is already stressed — but no single approval triggered a comprehensive review. Spotsylvania has 17 project sites. Each was reviewed separately. The official planning map confirming the full scale was only produced after most were already approved or in process.
5. Community Discovery Residents discover what is happening — often from a neighbor, a Facebook post, or a local reporter. By this point, multiple permits are already issued. The "Enjoy Stafford, Virginia" Facebook group. The "Aqua Virginia Customers Unite" group. The Caroline County water meetings. All reactive — after the contracts are signed.
6. The Lobbying Wall Companies deploy professional lobbyists at every level simultaneously. Bills that would protect communities are killed. Sen. Stuart: "They swarm like bees the minute they know what you're going to do." Stuart's moratorium bill failed 8-2. His water prioritization bill failed. Roem's siting bill has been in conference committee for months. $226,000/day federal lobbying continues.
7. The Extraction Facilities operate for years, drawing water and power. When better markets emerge — or water runs out — companies move on. Infrastructure stays. Water does not come back. Christina Lhamon (Stafford): "Just like the rust belt...move in, drain, and leave." The Eastern Virginia aquifer is projected to reach depletion stress by 2055.

✅ Real Wins — Communities That Actually Stopped It

$64 Billion Blocked. These Are the Documented Cases of What Worked.

🏆 Cascade Locks, Oregon — Port Authority Officials RECALLED
Voters recalled two Port Authority officials in June 2023 for supporting a $100 million data center project from Roadhouse Digital. The newly seated board canceled the project entirely in July 2023. Full stop. Project dead.
How they won: Electoral accountability — voted out officials who approved it, replaced them with opponents
🏆 Warrenton, Virginia — Entire Town Council Voted Out
In the November 2024 election, residents voted out every town council member who supported Amazon's proposed data center. The newly elected council — composed entirely of project opponents — now holds the mandate to block it.
How they won: Organized electoral campaign — made the data center vote the defining issue in a local election
🏆 Tucson, Arizona — $3.6 Billion Project Killed
In August 2025, the Tucson City Council voted unanimously to kill Project Blue — a $3.6 billion data center proposed for a 290-acre site. This is one of the largest single project cancellations by dollar value in the opposition movement.
How they won: Organized public testimony + zoning objections + council majority persuaded on environmental grounds
🏆 Fluvanna County, Virginia — Permanent Moratorium
Implemented a special-use permit requirement and effectively ended by-right data center development with a unanimous Board of Supervisors vote. No new data centers can be built without going through a full public hearing process.
How they won: Organized citizen testimony + persistent advocacy + county attorneys who understood zoning law
🏆 California — Ratepayer Protection Law SIGNED
Governor Newsom signed SB 57 into law on October 11, 2025. Directs the California Public Utilities Commission to study data center energy costs and their impact on ratepayers — creating the evidentiary foundation for future mandatory cost-shifting protections. Mid-Atlantic grid rates already jumping from $2.2 billion to $14.7 billion for power delivery — the cost shift is documented.
How they won: State Senate bill by Sen. Steve Padilla + documented rate impact data + utility commission accountability
🏆 Oklahoma — Ratepayer Protection Passed UNANIMOUSLY
The Oklahoma Senate unanimously passed the Data Center Consumer Ratepayer Protection Act of 2026 (HB 2992). Protects Oklahoma families and small businesses from bearing data center energy infrastructure costs. Oklahoma — a deeply conservative, business-friendly state — passed this unanimously. That is the definition of bipartisan consensus.
How they won: Bipartisan framing as consumer protection, not anti-tech — emphasizing ratepayer fairness over environmental concerns
🏆 FESTUS, MISSOURI — April 9, 2026 — MOST RECENT MAJOR WIN
One week after the city council approved a $6 billion CRG Clayco data center on 360 acres, voters in this town of 14,000 ousted ALL FOUR incumbent council members who approved it. Dan Moore (Ward 3), Karl Weekley (Ward 1), Allen Joseph McCarthy (Ward 2), and Rick Belleville (Ward 4) each ran on pro-transparency and anti-data center platforms. Voters also collected recall signatures against the Mayor at polling places. In neighboring Pacific, MO, the pro-data center mayor also lost. This is the most recent and most complete electoral accountability win in the national movement.
How they won: A Sunshine Law FOIA revealed officials called opponents "uneducated" internally — that document became the defining evidence of a captured local government. Electoral sweep 7 days after the vote.
🏆 Virginia Data Center Reform Coalition — 120+ Advocates at Capitol
More than 120 Virginians traveled to Richmond for the 2026 Lobby Day. The Coalition has grown to 50+ nonprofit organizations, HOAs, and community groups. They pushed the Four Pillars framework: Transparency, Oversight, Protections, Incentives. Multiple bills introduced on their agenda in 2026.
How they won (partially): Coalition building across partisan lines — environmental groups + HOAs + conservation organizations + farm groups

🏛️ Virginia — The Ground Zero State

The World's Largest Data Center Concentration. 47 Gigawatts Contracted Without a Public Vote. The Most Organized Opposition in the Country.

"Virginia is already home to the world's largest concentration of data centers, with an IT power load believed to be nearly three times greater than the next largest market in Beijing."
— Piedmont Environmental Council / Virginia Data Center Reform Coalition, January 2025
"Without any public review or oversight by the state, Dominion Energy has already contracted with data centers for a startling 47 gigawatts of electricity — which nearly triples its current peak energy capacity and is the equivalent of more than 29 nuclear power plants."
— PEC Four Pillars of Data Center Reform, January 2025

What those 47 gigawatts mean for you: Dominion used those contracts to justify building new gas plants and delaying coal plant retirements — locking in fossil fuel infrastructure that ratepayers will pay for over decades. The Virginia DEQ has permitted thousands of diesel generators as backup power for data centers. Onsite gas turbines are now being approved as primary power. None of this went to a public vote.

The Four Pillars Virginia's Reform Coalition Is Demanding — All Four Are Reasonable

1. TRANSPARENCY — Require Disclosure
Require local disclosure and statewide reporting on data center energy use, water consumption, and emissions. Without standardized reporting, cumulative impacts cannot be assessed. SB553/HB496 (water disclosure bills currently in conference) are the legislative vehicle for this pillar.
2. OVERSIGHT — State-Level Review
Establish state-level regulatory review in addition to local review — to evaluate far-reaching regional impacts that cross jurisdictional lines. The Mattameade campus crossing Spotsylvania/Caroline county lines is the clearest example of why county-by-county review is structurally inadequate.
3. PROTECTIONS — Stop Cost-Shifting to Ratepayers
Prevent residents and businesses from subsidizing data center energy infrastructure. Oklahoma and Ohio have already passed this. California is studying it. Virginia has not acted. The Mid-Atlantic grid capacity auction jumped from $2.2B to $14.7B — that cost lands on all ratepayers, not just data center operators.
4. INCENTIVES — Tie Tax Exemptions to Performance
Connect Virginia's data center sales and use tax exemption to clean energy and efficiency standards. Currently, any data center qualifies regardless of environmental performance. The tax exemption sunset is currently in 2035 — Stuart's bills to accelerate this have faced the lobbyist wall.
Source: PEC Virginia, "Four Pillars of Data Center Reform," January 8, 2025, updated February 2026 (pecva.org/work/energy-work/data-centers/four-pillars-of-data-center-reform/).
⚔️ What Actually Works — Documented by Results

Not Theory. These Are the Tactics That Have Produced the $64 Billion in Blocked Projects.

🔴 TIER 1 — Electoral Accountability (Highest Impact)
  • Vote out officials who approve projects over community objection. Cascade Locks, Oregon did it. Warrenton, Virginia did it. Data Center Watch documents this as the tactic the industry fears most — because it removes their captured officials permanently.
  • Make the data center vote the single defining issue in local elections. Not "anti-development" — frame it as "did this official protect our water and ratepayers?" That framing works across party lines.
  • Document every vote, every official, every approval. Build the public record so voters can see exactly who approved what.
🔴 TIER 1 — Regulatory Intervention (Highest Legal Impact)
  • File to intervene in every SCC proceeding. The NextEra/Dominion merger, the Aqua rate case, the Essential/American Water merger — all require SCC approval and all accept public intervenors. Intervenors get to submit evidence, cross-examine witnesses, and file legal briefs. This is the most powerful legal tool available to ordinary people.
  • File comments in every DEQ water permit proceeding. The Caroline County 13.9 MGD Rappahannock permit is still suspended. Written comments supporting a residential-only modification are on the record.
  • FOIA the commissioners' financial disclosures. Virginia Ethics Council, 804-225-3138. If Bagot, Towell, or Hudson own stock in Essential Utilities, NextEra, or Digital Realty — that is a story that changes the political dynamics of the rate case and merger proceedings.

The federal order creates pressure but does not eliminate the legal tools that communities have successfully used to block $64 billion in projects.
