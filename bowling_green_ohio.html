Section 3 — Legal Precedents & Case Law

Other Communities Have Already Been to Court. Use Their Roadmap.

Hilliard v. Amazon / AEP — Ohio ERAC Appeal (Active, 2025–2026)

City of Hilliard filed an appeal with Ohio's Environmental Review Appeals Commission challenging the air permit for Amazon's data center fuel cell system. Argument: Ohio EPA failed to give proper statutory notice to local government. The city argued that ORC 4906.13(B) cannot override the constitutional right of municipalities to protect their residents' health and safety. Norwich Township joined as a supporting party. This case is your direct playbook.

ORC 4906.10(A)(6) — "Public Interest, Convenience, and Necessity" Requirement

Every Ohio Power Siting Board approval must demonstrate the facility "will serve the public interest, convenience, and necessity." Community members can formally challenge whether a 350MW private gas plant serving one corporation — while sitting next to a municipal water facility — meets this standard. This is the argument that cracked open the Hilliard case.

Saline Township, Michigan — Zoning Injunction (2025–2026)

Saline Township residents voted down an OpenAI/Oracle data center. Developers went ahead anyway. The township obtained a temporary restraining order based on the argument that state economic development authority cannot override local zoning for non-utility private development. The case established that community consent matters in siting decisions — even when developers have state approval. Michigan federal court. Cite this in every Ohio filing.

Safe Drinking Water Act — 42 U.S.C. § 300f et seq.

If any of the 54 wells within one mile of the Apollo facility are private drinking water wells and construction or operation degrades water quality, this creates a federal cause of action. The EPA's own permit language states the project is "not expected" to impact wells — but that is a prediction, not a guarantee. Document every change in well water quality with dated photos, lab tests, and written reports. Each documented change is evidence for a federal SDWA claim.

Great Lakes Compact — ORC Chapter 1522

Ohio is a signatory to the Great Lakes–St. Lawrence River Basin Water Resources Compact. Any water withdrawal exceeding 100,000 gallons per day requires a permit and a finding of no significant adverse impact to the Basin. A hyperscale data center withdrawing millions of gallons daily must comply. Challenge whether proper Great Lakes Compact review was conducted. The Alliance for the Great Lakes is already engaged on this issue statewide.

NEPA — National Environmental Policy Act (Federal Trigger)

If any federal permits, federal funding, federal land, or federal interconnection approvals were involved in this project, NEPA review is required. This includes any involvement by FERC (Federal Energy Regulatory Commission) in grid interconnection, any USDA Rural Development funding in the area, or any federal highway/infrastructure impact. File a FOIA with every federal agency that may have touched this project. One federal nexus triggers full NEPA review.

Constitutional — Due Process / Notice Requirement

The Apollo facility was approved under an "expedited" process that held no public hearings. Communities have a due process right to meaningful notice and opportunity to be heard before major industrial facilities affecting their health and safety are sited near their homes. The Hilliard case raises this exact argument. 14 people submitted comments and all were ignored — that is a fact pattern supporting a procedural due process challenge.
