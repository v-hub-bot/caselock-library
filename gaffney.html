💧 Gaffney's Water — What the Testing Shows

Gaffney Board of Public Works — What's in the Water

The Gaffney Board of Public Works (BPW) is the local utility providing water, wastewater, and electric service to Gaffney and surrounding Cherokee County areas. The BPW commissioners are elected by customers — which is structurally different from Aqua Virginia's investor-owned model. But the water quality issues are documented.

Texas-based Cielo Digital Infrastructure announced on June 24, 2025 that it had selected Cherokee County for its first South Carolina development project. The announcement came jointly from Cielo, Governor Henry McMaster, Secretary of Commerce Harry M. Lightsey III, and Cherokee County Council Chairman Tim Spencer.

The 30-Jobs Problem: A $2.1 billion investment is expected to create 30 permanent jobs. That is $70 million of capital investment per job created. Data centers are capital-intensive, not labor-intensive. For comparison, a $2.1 billion automotive plant would typically create 1,500–3,000 jobs. The economic case being made to Cherokee County residents rests almost entirely on tax revenue — not employment. And that tax revenue calculus depends entirely on what tax incentives the state and county gave away to attract the project, which has not been fully disclosed.

Cherokee County's Response — Setting Limits

Following the Cielo announcement, Cherokee County moved to set conditions on data center development. Local residents raised concerns specifically about water and power infrastructure capacity. The county reportedly required that incoming data centers provide their own power infrastructure rather than drawing from existing residential capacity — a direct response to community concern.

The Water Disclosure Gap — Documented Statewide:
A Facebook post from a South Carolina community group stated directly: "South Carolina doesn't know how much water the data center industry uses. They don't get a water bill? I get a water bill."

That is not hyperbole. South Carolina Bill 724 (2025–2026 session) was introduced specifically because there was no existing requirement for data centers to report water usage to the state. SC Bill 724 would require data centers using 3 million or more gallons per month to report to the Department of Environmental Services. As of this writing, it has not been enacted into law.

In Georgia, a data center drained 30 million gallons of water unnoticed — the details only emerged in a May 2025 letter from the county water system after the fact. South Carolina has no mechanism to prevent the same thing from happening in Cherokee County.
Sources: SC Bill 724 (scstatehouse.gov/sess126_2025-2026/bills/724.htm); SC Bill 867; Politico, May 8, 2026 — "A data center drained 30M gallons of water unnoticed"; SC Policy Council data center reform reporting.

The BPW Confirmation — and the Unanswered Question

City leaders asked the Gaffney Board of Public Works whether power and water infrastructure is sufficient to accommodate the increase in data center use. The BPW confirmed infrastructure adequacy — but that confirmation was made before full water usage figures for the Cielo campus were publicly disclosed. The capacity analysis has not been made public in detail.

⚖️ The SC PSC — Same Structure as Virginia's SCC, Same Problem

Who Appoints the Regulators in South Carolina

Virginia's SCC commissioners are elected by the General Assembly. South Carolina's PSC commissioners are also elected by the General Assembly. This is not common. Only Virginia and South Carolina use this model — every other state either elects commissioners by popular vote or has the governor appoint them.

1
The SC PSC has 7 commissioners — one from each U.S. Congressional House District. They regulate rates and services of all investor-owned public utilities in South Carolina — including Duke Energy and Dominion/Santee Cooper. SC PSC FAQ; Ballotpedia; SC Code of Laws
2
Commissioners are elected by the South Carolina General Assembly in a joint session, to staggered 4-year terms. The Public Utilities Review Committee screens candidates and nominates up to three per seat before the full General Assembly votes. SC PSC FAQ (psc.sc.gov); Ballotpedia; scstatehouse.gov
3
The most recent election was March 4, 2026. Incoming commissioners: Swain Whitfield and Eugene Hennelly. Reelected: Chair Delton Powers and Commissioner Mike Caston. SC Utility Consumer Office, March 5, 2026
4
The same General Assembly members who elect these commissioners also receive campaign contributions from the utilities those commissioners regulate — Duke Energy, Dominion, and others. This is the same structural conflict documented in Virginia with Dominion and Aqua/Essential Utilities. Post and Courier; SC Policy Council; Energy and Policy Institute
5
PSC commissioners are legally prohibited from talking to the public about pending cases — classified as a judicial function, meaning ex parte communication rules apply. A commissioner cannot discuss a rate case with a ratepayer even if they want to. Documented in SC community group posts referencing PSC procedural rules

💥 The Nukegate Scandal — South Carolina's Proof the System Is Broken

$9 Billion Billed to Ratepayers. Executives Went to Prison. Legislators Were Already Funded.

The V.C. Summer nuclear project — and the resulting "Nukegate" scandal — is the most documented case of South Carolina utility corruption in state history. It directly involves the same PSC structure that governs your utility bills today.

What Happened — The Core Facts

  • South Carolina Electric & Gas (SCE&G, owned by SCANA Corporation) and Santee Cooper collectively invested $9 billion into building two new nuclear reactors at V.C. Summer Nuclear Station.
  • The project was abandoned in 2017 — after ratepayers had already been billed billions through a mechanism called Base Load Review Act (BLRA) charges that allowed utilities to charge customers before the plant was even built.
  • SCANA CEO Kevin Marsh and Vice President Stephen Byrne were charged by the U.S. Department of Justice for fraud — specifically for lying about construction progress to maintain the $1.4 billion federal production tax credit and to keep charging customers.
  • Byrne was sentenced to 15 months in federal prison. Marsh pleaded guilty. Both admitted to fraud.
  • The SEC separately charged SCANA, SCE&G, and the two executives for defrauding investors by concealing construction problems.
The Legislative Money Connection — Documented by the Post and Courier:
The Post and Courier reported directly: "SCANA has given money to nearly all South Carolina lawmakers probing the failed nuclear project." (September 2017)

Read that again: the legislators assigned to investigate SCANA for the nuclear fraud had already received money from SCANA. The same company under criminal investigation was funding the people investigating it — through the same legislative donation system that also funds the legislators who elect the PSC commissioners who approved SCANA's rates for decades.

What Ratepayers Are STILL Paying

As of 2024, South Carolina power customers are still paying off $2.3 billion in debt from the failed nuclear project. This fee does not appear as a line item on monthly bills — it is embedded in the rate structure. Customers have been paying for reactors that were never built, never will be built, and whose construction executives went to federal prison for lying about. SC Daily Gazette, April 5, 2024

Two PSC Commissioners Lost Their Seats After Nukegate

Two members of the SC PSC lost their positions in the aftermath of Nukegate — but not through any criminal process. They were simply not reelected by the General Assembly. That is the only accountability mechanism available. The same General Assembly that was receiving money from SCANA. The same body that elects the commissioners who approved SCANA's rates.

Sources: Post and Courier; nuclearfreenw.org/summer-criminal-investigations.pdf; SC Daily Gazette April 2024; southcarolinapublicradio.org March 2023; en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nukegate_scandal.

📋 South Carolina Bills — What's Being Fought Over Right Now

The Legislative Fights That Affect Cherokee County Directly

BillWhat It DoesStatus
SC Bill 724
Commercial Data Center Water Usage Reporting		 Requires data centers using 3 million+ gallons of water per month to report to the SC Department of Environmental Services. Would create the first-ever water usage disclosure requirement for the data center industry in South Carolina. Pending — not enacted as of May 2026
SC Bill 867
Data Center Development Standards		 Requires data centers to implement "reasonable measures" to minimize noise, vibration, and light impacts. Includes provisions for water conservation, energy efficiency, and environmental stewardship. Includes a closed-loop cooling system requirement. Pending — not enacted as of May 2026
SC Bill 902
Data Center Operational Efficiency Standards		 Establishes "operational efficiency standards" for data center water use, cooling technologies, and energy use. Defines standards as "technology-neutral requirements." Pending — not enacted as of May 2026
SC Budget Proviso (2026)
Water Reporting (Watered Down)		 The SC Senate added a budget proviso requiring water usage reporting from commercial data centers for this fiscal year only. SC Policy Council described this as the Senate "watering down data center reform" — a temporary, weak substitute for the permanent legislation. Passed as budget proviso only — not permanent law. SC Policy Council criticized as insufficient.
The Gap That Exists Right Now: The Cielo data center in Gaffney is expected to begin operations by 2028. None of the permanent water disclosure or environmental standards bills have been enacted. The budget proviso is a one-year fix. By the time these facilities are operational, the regulatory framework — if it passes at all — may barely cover them. A Georgia data center drained 30 million gallons of water unnoticed. South Carolina has no permanent mechanism to prevent the same thing from happening on Ford Road in Gaffney.

Specific Tools Available in South Carolina Right Now

  • SC Utility Consumer Office: The state has a dedicated Office of Regulatory Staff (ORS) that represents consumer interests in PSC proceedings. Contact them: ORS, 1401 Main Street, Suite 900, Columbia, SC 29201. Phone: 803-737-5100. Website: ors.sc.gov. File formal comments on any pending Duke Energy rate case.
  • PSC Formal Intervention: Any member of the public can petition to formally intervene in a utility rate case at the SC PSC — giving you party status and the right to submit evidence. Contact the SC PSC: 101 Executive Center Drive, Suite 100, Columbia, SC 29210. Phone: 803-896-5100.
  • Support Bills 724, 867, 902: Contact your SC House member and State Senator directly and ask for their position on data center water disclosure legislation. Ask specifically: "Will you support requiring the Cielo data center on Ford Road in Cherokee County to disclose how much water it uses?"
  • FOIA the Water Agreement: If the Gaffney BPW or Cherokee County signed any agreement with Cielo regarding water supply, that agreement may be subject to the South Carolina Freedom of Information Act (SC Code § 30-4-10 et seq.). File a FOIA request directly with Cherokee County and with the BPW.
  • SC DHEC / SCDES Environmental Complaints: South Carolina Department of Environmental Services (formerly DHEC) accepts formal complaints regarding water quality and infrastructure. File at: des.sc.gov. Document everything — dates, test results, advisories.
  • Connect with the Luck Stone Cherokee Quarry Fight: A separate environmental fight is also active in Cherokee County — a public hearing was held September 18, 2025 on the proposed Cherokee Quarry. These fights share community members and institutional knowledge.
