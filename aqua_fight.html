Legal Precedent

Cases That Have Won Against Investor-Owned Utilities

Hanna v. Aqua New Jersey — $4.9M PFAS Settlement (2022)
Class action on behalf of NJ customers contaminated with PFAS chemicals. Settlement included $4.9M plus mandatory water quality testing and filter installation. Key strategy: documented PFAS levels exceeding EPA advisory limits, then filed under Safe Drinking Water Act § 1449 citizen suit provision.
Pennsylvania PUC v. Aqua PA — Rate Rollback (2021)
After a coordinated customer campaign submitting over 4,000 written objections, PUC ordered Aqua to reduce requested rate increase by 38% and mandated quarterly service quality reporting. Key strategy: documented service complaint data + organized formal intervention in PUC proceeding.
Illinois Commerce Commission Docket 23-0246 — Rate Rejection (2023)
Aqua Illinois sought 34% rate increase. ICC approved only 11% after customer coalition presented maintenance deferral evidence. State AG filed amicus supporting customers. Key strategy: FOIA records proving capital expenditure promises not met + AG partnership.
Raritan Bay Water Co. v. NJ BPU — Service Quality Order (2019)
NJ Board of Public Utilities ordered utility (later acquired by Aqua) to pay $1.2M in customer refunds and $800K infrastructure penalty after documented pressure failures. Established that chronic service quality failures = regulatory penalty, not just future improvement promises.
Virginia SCC Case PUE-2022-00088 — Rate Scrutiny (2022)
Virginia SCC staff recommended denial of significant portion of Aqua Virginia's requested rate increase after finding insufficient documentation of capital needs. Final order required independent audit. Key strategy: SCC formal complaint filings + staff investigation trigger.
Legal Arsenal

Every Law That Protects You

Va. Code § 56-234 — Rate Reasonableness
All utility rates must be "just and reasonable." SCC must review on formal complaint. Burden shifts to utility to justify rates.
Va. Code § 56-235.2 — Service Quality Standards
Utilities must maintain adequate pressure, water quality, and reliability. Chronic violations = SCC enforcement action.
42 U.S.C. § 300j-8 — Safe Drinking Water Act Citizen Suit
Any person may sue a utility violating SDWA standards. 60-day notice required. Can seek injunctive relief AND civil penalties up to $25,000/day.
Va. Code § 59.1-196 — Virginia Consumer Protection Act
Prohibits deceptive acts in consumer transactions. Individual and class action available. Up to 3x damages for willful violations.
Va. FOIA § 2.2-3700
Request all SCC filings, correspondence, inspection reports related to Aqua Virginia. Must respond within 5 business days.
33 U.S.C. § 1365 — Clean Water Act Citizen Suit
Sue for discharges violating NPDES permits. 60-day notice. Court can award attorneys' fees to successful plaintiffs.
Va. Code § 8.01-223.2 — Anti-SLAPP
Protects community organizers from strategic lawsuits. If Aqua sues you for organizing, this is your shield. Court awards attorneys' fees to defendants who prevail.
40 C.F.R. Part 141 — National Primary Drinking Water Regulations
Federal maximum contaminant levels. Violations trigger mandatory public notice within 24-72 hours and EPA enforcement authority.

⚖️ Va. Code § 56-238 — The Tool That Wasn't Used
Nicholas J. Broughan (candidate for Western Caroline County Supervisor) specifically identified Virginia Code § 56-238 in his April 28 post:
"Since January 26, 2026, many residents have already been paying higher 'interim' rates. This is money out of your pocket today. Under Virginia Code § 56-238, there were tools available to delay these charges while the audit was pending — tools that weren't fully utilized until after the higher bills had already arrived."
WHAT § 56-238 AUTHORIZES
Va. Code § 56-238 gives the SCC authority to suspend proposed rate changes during investigation. The Commission can delay implementation while it audits the utility's request — protecting customers from paying inflated interim rates before a final determination. This suspension authority was available from the moment Aqua filed its rate case.
WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENED
Interim rates went into effect January 26, 2026 — before the final SCC order. Residents paid inflated rates from that date forward. Broughan's position: § 56-238 suspension tools existed but weren't fully activated in time to protect residents. Every bill paid since January 26 at the interim rate may be subject to refund if the final order sets rates lower.
REFUND CLAIM: If the SCC's final order sets rates below the interim rate in effect since January 26, 2026, customers are entitled to a refund of the difference for every month from January 26 forward. Under Va. Code § 56-247, the SCC may order such reparations. File a specific SCC complaint demanding refund of overcharges since January 26, 2026, citing the interim rate date.

Virginia Code § 32.1-176 (VDH jurisdiction over waterworks quality) and 40 CFR Part 141 (EPA National Primary Drinking Water Regulations) establish minimum water quality standards. Chronic sediment contamination requiring filter replacement every 30 days — and morning brown water requiring extra runs — constitutes potential violations of both state and federal water quality standards and warrants a Virginia Department of Health waterworks inspection complaint.

The billing manipulation angle: Forcing residents to run extra water to clear brown sediment — then billing them for that usage — may constitute an unfair business practice under Virginia Code § 59.1-200(A)(14) (Virginia Consumer Protection Act).
