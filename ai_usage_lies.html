What Futurism/Rolling Stone Reported — Attributed to Named Sources

  • Location: Morrow County, eastern Oregon. Population ~45,000. Amazon data center first went online there in 2011. 10,000 square foot facility.
  • What Jim Doherty (cattle rancher, former county commissioner) found: Surveyed 70 wells — 68 violated the federal nitrate limit for drinking water. Of the first 30 homes visited, 25 residents had recently had miscarriages. Six had lost a kidney. Per Rolling Stone: "One man about 60 years old had his voice box taken out because of a cancer that only smokers get, but that guy hadn't smoked a day of his life."
  • The mechanism alleged: Industrial megafarms produce millions of gallons of wastewater laden with nitrates from fertilizers. Amazon's data center added millions of gallons of additional wastewater per year, supercharging an already stressed system. The aquifer became contaminated. The data center then drew contaminated groundwater to cool its chips. Evaporation concentrated the contaminants — in some cases to nitrate levels 8x higher than Oregon's safe limit. That super-concentrated water then re-entered the waste system.
  • Amazon's response (spokesperson Lisa Levandowski): "Our data centers draw water from the same supply as other community members; nitrates are not an additive we use in any of our processes, and the volume of water our facilities use and return represents only a very small fraction of the overall water system — not enough to have any meaningful impact on water quality."
  • Kristin Ostrom, Executive Director, Oregon Rural Action: "The historical precedent here is Flint, Michigan. In part because of how slow the response to the crisis has been, and in part because of who's affected. These are people who have no political or economic power, and very little knowledge of the risk."
  • Area resident Kathy Mendoza told Rolling Stone she is suffering a joint and muscle condition she attributes to nitrate exposure.

🔬 Closed Loop Cooling — What They Are Selling You vs. What the Chemistry Actually Does

"Yes, They Waste Less Water — But When Their Wastewater Is Released It Might Poison Your Entire Region"

The speaker's point about closed-loop systems is verified by peer-reviewed industrial chemistry analysis. KETOS (an industrial water monitoring company) published a detailed technical analysis titled "Closed-Loop Cooling: Water Saver or Chemical Time Bomb?" — here is what it confirmed:

Chemical AdditiveTypical Concentration in Closed LoopSafe Water LimitThe Problem
Nitrite corrosion inhibitor 600–1,200 ppm (steel); up to 7,000 ppm in mixed-metal circuits 1 ppm in U.S. drinking water Converts to carcinogenic nitrosamines at wastewater treatment plants
Glycol antifreeze 25–50% by volume Discharge cap <0.25% (University of Michigan standard) Spills spike biochemical oxygen demand (BOD) — one airport glycol runoff event = sewage load of 176,000 people
Copper and zinc (from pipe metals) Hundreds to thousands µg/L Copper cap 4,000 µg/L Toxic to aquatic life; triggers extra treatment plant fees
  • The "blow-down" problem: Closed loops recirculate the same water repeatedly. Contaminants — salts, metals, treatment chemicals — build up with each recycling round. Operators "bleed" a fraction of water monthly to manage this. That bleed, when it leaves the site, can carry nitrite, glycol, and heavy metals at concentrations thousands of times above surface water limits.
  • Unplanned events: A single 500 m³ loop drained for repair can send over 3 tonnes of nitrite to the local treatment plant. Valve leaks to storm drains — glycol above 10% concentration suffocates aquatic life faster than raw sewage.
  • Most facilities test only quarterly — but loop chemistry can swing dangerously within hours if oxygen enters or microbes oxidize nitrite.
  • The Morrow County mechanism confirmed: The scenario described in the Amazon/Oregon case — concentrated wastewater re-entering the local water system — is exactly what KETOS's analysis identifies as the core risk of closed-loop systems at scale.
Andrew Fox's comment (from this TikTok thread) is technically accurate: "The closed loop and concrete hold water from ever entering the water cycle. This will make the droughts worse and we won't be able to keep the water table and aquifers where they need to be without the regular rain of 20 years ago." The 80% evaporation rate from open cooling towers permanently removes water from the local water cycle. Closed-loop systems evaporate less — but what they discharge when they bleed or drain carries concentrated chemical loads. Neither system is benign at the scale of dozens of gigawatt campuses.
