🚨 THE CORE FACT THEY WON'T TELL YOU AT THE MEETING:
Up to 85% of the water a data center uses evaporates and never returns to your aquifer. It goes into the atmosphere as steam. Your town's groundwater goes in — and most of it never comes back. This is verified by the Missouri Policy Initiative and multiple university water studies.

WHAT IS A "CONE OF DEPRESSION" AND WHY YOUR NEIGHBORS' WELLS MATTER

When a large facility pumps massive volumes of water from a local aquifer, it creates what scientists call a "cone of depression" — a funnel-shaped drop in the surrounding water table. Private wells within that cone can go dry within weeks or months. Municipal wells drawing from the same aquifer see reduced pressure and output.

⚠️ FOR SMALL ILLINOIS TOWNS: If your town is on a shallow drift aquifer (most rural towns are), a data center drawing even 500,000 gallons per day can pull the water table below the intake level of residential wells within a 1–2 mile radius. Once those wells go dry, you're on bottled water or you're paying to drill deeper — at your own expense.
THE "THEY'LL RETURN THE WATER" MYTH — DEBUNKED
Common claim from data center developers: "We return wastewater to the system."

The truth: Cooling towers work by evaporation. The water is intentionally turned into vapor to carry heat away from the servers. That vapor rises into the atmosphere. It does not fall back into your local aquifer — it joins the regional weather system and may fall as rain hundreds of miles away. The "return" is to a distant watershed, not yours.

WHAT TO DEMAND AT THE MEETING SPECIFICALLY ON WATER
🎤 Say this at the May 26th meeting: "I need this board to answer three specific questions about water before any permit is considered: One — What aquifer system does this proposed site draw from, and what is the current documented water table level in that aquifer? We need a baseline on the record before any withdrawal begins. Two — How many gallons per day will this facility withdraw, and what percentage is projected to be returned to the local aquifer versus lost to evaporation? Cooling tower evaporation typically removes 80–85% of withdrawn water permanently. Three — Has a 'cone of depression' impact study been conducted showing the radius of water table depression for private wells and municipal intakes within two miles of this site? If any of these three questions cannot be answered tonight, this board does not have sufficient information to approve anything. I am requesting those studies be completed and made public before any vote."

Apply the same logic to your local aquifer.

WHAT ILLINOIS GIVES YOU THAT OTHER STATES DON'T
✅ KEY FACT: Illinois has some of the strongest local zoning authority in the Midwest. Your town's zoning board and village/city council have real power here — more than they probably realize.
LEGAL TOOLWHAT IT DOESHOW TO USE IT
Illinois Municipal Code — 65 ILCS 5/11-13Gives municipalities full authority to regulate land use, including industrial facilities, through zoning ordinancesDemand the board produce the current zoning classification for the proposed site. Illinois Administrative Procedure ActAny permit issued without following proper procedure is subject to administrative appealDocument every step. If they skip a required step, you have grounds to challenge

THE TAX BREAK PROBLEM — ILLINOIS SB 2858
⚠️ CRITICAL: Illinois law currently gives data centers a full sales tax exemption on equipment purchases — worth hundreds of millions of dollars. This means the facility gets massive state subsidies while your town absorbs all the costs (roads, water, power, emergency services). Your town gets none of that tax money back. Say this at the meeting.
