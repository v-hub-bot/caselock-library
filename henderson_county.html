📍 The Situation — What Is Happening in Henderson County

A Major AI Data Center Proposed in Trinidad, Texas — Directly Adjacent to Cedar Creek Reservoir

Henderson County is in East Texas, southeast of Dallas. Cedar Creek Reservoir is a major water supply serving the region. A large-scale AI data center has been proposed for the Trinidad area, near Cedar Creek Lake and Key Ranch Road — immediately adjacent to the reservoir that the surrounding community depends on for drinking water.

📋 THE RESOLUTION — Exact Language — This Is the Template Other Counties Are Using

Henderson County Commissioners Court Resolution — May 12, 2026 — Read Into Public Record by County Attorney Kenneth Strawn

"The Henderson County Commissioners Court supports responsible economic development, including appropriately designed and sited data center facilities. However, formally expresses opposition to using open loop evaporative cooling systems or other high volume potable water consumption technologies in large scale, high continuous load data center facilities within water constrained regions absent demonstrated sustainable supply and mitigation safeguards.

The court further opposes any data center development that fails to incorporate adequate safeguards to protect county resources and residents.

The court further states that support for additional data center development in the state of Texas should be conditioned upon the implementation of comprehensive, enforceable safeguards to protect the electric grid reliability and prevent cost burdens on residential, agricultural and small business ratepayers."
Why this resolution language matters nationally: This is not a ban. It is a conditions-based opposition — the county supports development that meets specific standards and opposes development that doesn't. This framing is legally stronger than a blanket "no data centers" position because it survives property rights challenges. It directly targets the open-loop evaporative cooling technology responsible for the highest water consumption. The "water constrained regions" language creates a documented factual test. The "cost burdens on residential, agricultural and small business ratepayers" language mirrors what Oklahoma and California have passed into law. This resolution was drafted by the County Attorney and read into the public record — it is a model document that can be adapted for any county in Texas, Virginia, or any other state.

Commissioner Spivey's clarification (on the record): "This is not a vote approving or denying any specific project today. It's just a statement that economic development and protecting our community should go hand in hand." — This framing protects the county from being sued for denying a specific application, while still creating a documented policy position on the record.

💧 The Water Numbers — Texas-Specific, All From Official Sources

Texas Is the State Where the Water Math Is Most Alarming

  • 49 billion gallons — Texas data center water consumption in 2025 (HARC/University of Houston study, cited Lincoln Institute of Land Policy)
  • 399 billion gallons — projected Texas data center water use by 2030 — including indirect power generation water (HARC/University of Houston) — equivalent to drawing down Lake Mead 16 feet in a single year
  • 3–9% of Texas total water demand by 2040 — University of Texas at Austin study cited at the Henderson County hearing (KLTV, May 12, 2026)
  • 5 million gallons per day — maximum daily water consumption of a large data center, per EESI (Environmental and Energy Study Institute), cited at the hearing
  • Equivalent to a town of 10,000 to 50,000 people — 5 million gallons per day put in residential context (EESI via KLTV)
  • 400+ data centers operating or under construction in Texas as of May 2026 (KLTV)
  • Cedar Creek Reservoir is a freshwater reservoir — the data center would draw from the same watershed that municipal water systems depend on
The geographic conflict: The proposed data center in the KLTV map is shown directly adjacent to Cedar Creek Reservoir with the label "FUTURE DATA CENTER" overlaid on the reservoir itself. A 5 million gallon per day facility next to a major reservoir in a region already identified as water-constrained represents exactly the cumulative impact scenario that the University of Illinois Center for Secure Water (January 2026) warned about — permit-by-permit approval that doesn't assess the cumulative watershed impact until thresholds are already crossed.
Sources: HARC/University of Houston study (harcresearch.org); Lincoln Institute of Land Policy "Data Drain"; EESI data center water consumption analysis; University of Texas at Austin study cited in KLTV reporting; KLTV map showing Cedar Creek Reservoir and proposed data center location.
🔗 Connection to the Virginia and National Fight

Henderson County Is Doing What Virginia Communities Have Been Demanding — On the Record

The Henderson County resolution mirrors language that Virginia community advocates and the Data Center Reform Coalition have been pushing for — specifically:

  • Opposition to open-loop evaporative cooling = the 80% evaporation rate we've documented for Rappahannock/Mattaponi watershed impacts
  • Ratepayer protection language = what Oklahoma passed unanimously and California signed into law in October 2025
  • Request for Special Session = equivalent to Virginia's water disclosure bills (SB553/HB496) — state-level intervention demanded by local communities
  • Wise County, Texas has passed a nearly identical resolution — confirming this is becoming a standard Texas county-level response

The Henderson County resolution language — particularly the "open loop evaporative cooling in water constrained regions" prohibition — can be directly adapted for any Virginia county facing a data center permit application.
