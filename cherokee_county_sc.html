The Evaporation Problem: Published research (EESI, Missouri Policy Initiative) confirms that evaporative cooling towers expel 80–85% of withdrawn water permanently into the atmosphere — it does not return to the Broad River or local groundwater. Only 15–20% is returned. This is not "water recycling." SC's own State Water Plan notes: "Some data centers use 80 percent of the water withdrawn, returning only about 20 percent to rivers."
Source: The State (SC), SC DES 2025 Water Plan; EESI; Lincoln Institute; Missouri Policy Initiative

🌊 Aquifer & Groundwater Risk — Piedmont Region

What the Ground Holds — And What It Can't

Cherokee County sits in the Piedmont physiographic region of South Carolina — above the fall line. This is critical: unlike the coastal plain aquifer system, Piedmont groundwater is stored primarily in fractured crystalline rock (metamorphic and igneous bedrock). These aquifers have:

CharacteristicPiedmont Fractured Rock AquiferImpact
Storage typeFractures and joints in crystalline rock — NOT porous sedimentVery limited storage compared to coastal plain
Recharge rateSlow — depends on precipitation infiltrating through thin soilsCannot recover quickly from heavy pumping
Well yieldsTypically 1–20 gallons per minute for domestic wellsIndustrial demand (thousands of GPM) can quickly overwhelm local wells
Cone of depression riskHIGH — SC State Water Plan documents existing cones of depression in inland areasHeavy pumping pulls down water table in radius around extraction point
Interconnection with surface waterDirectly connected — Broad River levels affect groundwater and vice versaReduced river flow = reduced groundwater recharge
What a "Cone of Depression" Means for You: The SC State Water Plan specifically notes that "some inland areas face cones of depression, where groundwater levels have dropped and utilities are drawing from other aquifers." If Cielo's campus begins extracting large volumes of water — directly or through the municipal system — nearby private wells in Blacksburg and Cherokee County could experience declining water tables. This is not a prediction — it is a documented risk pattern identified in the state's own plan for this exact region type.
Source: SC Post and Courier Dec 12, 2025 (citing SC DES Water Plan); SC DES 2025 State Water Plan; USGS Piedmont aquifer documentation
NO BASELINE EXISTS: There is no publicly available pre-development groundwater baseline study for the Ford Road site or surrounding Cherokee County properties. Without a baseline, if your well fails or drops after the campus opens, you cannot demonstrate a connection to the data center's water use. Now — before construction begins — is the time to document your well depth and water level.
