The Structural Problem — Documented by Multiple Sources

Virginia is one of the only states where the General Assembly — not the governor — elects utility regulators. The SCC's three commissioners are chosen by a vote of the House and Senate sitting in joint session. Dominion Energy donates to those same legislators. The legislators then vote to seat the regulators who decide Dominion's rate increases.

"A year after Dominion Energy suffered its biggest legislative loss in decades, Virginia's largest utility is back as the most powerful political force in Richmond."
— Virginia Mercury, February 27, 2024

The Energy and Policy Institute documented in January 2024 that legislators who received Dominion contributions voted to elect Kelsey Bagot and Samuel Towell to the SCC. The vote was unanimous — both parties. The Virginia Mercury confirmed the Democratic-controlled General Assembly certified both candidates in January 2024.

The Direct Connection to Your Water Bill: The SCC commissioners who approve Aqua Virginia's rate increases are elected by legislators funded by Dominion Energy — the company whose merger is now pending before those same commissioners. Commissioner Bagot came directly from NextEra — the company buying Dominion. Commissioner Towell was elected by those Dominion-funded legislators. This is the documented chain, sourced from VPAP public records, the Virginia Mercury, Energy and Policy Institute, and Clean Virginia.

⚠️ NextEra / Florida Power & Light — The Documented Record

This Is the Company About to Own Virginia's Electric Grid. Here Is What Courts, Journalists, and Federal Investigators Have Documented.

All items below are sourced from court records, federal agency filings, published journalism (Miami Herald, Orlando Sentinel, Floodlight News), or SEC documents. Nothing here is CaseLock's independent allegation.

The Ghost Candidate Scandal — 2020–2023
Florida Power & Light (FPL), NextEra's principal subsidiary, had senior executives closely connected to a scheme that placed "ghost candidates" — fake third-party candidates — in Florida state Senate races to siphon votes from FPL's political opponents. The Orlando Sentinel (December 2021) reported that FPL executives worked closely with the consultants who ran the ghost candidate scheme. Three people faced state charges. A Republican consultant's deposition (Floodlight News) revealed the scheme was designed specifically to defeat a Florida Senate candidate who was a political opponent of FPL. FPL denied providing direct funding, but the connections between its executives and the scheme's organizers were documented in public records. No FPL or NextEra employees were criminally charged as of January 2024 (Energy and Policy Institute).
CEO Eric Silagy — Resignation and Federal Scrutiny — 2023
FPL CEO Eric Silagy abruptly resigned in 2023 amid what the Miami Herald described as "federal scrutiny." NextEra's own CEO James Robo confirmed in a call with Bank of America analysts that NextEra was internally investigating the dark money and ghost candidate connections. Silagy made $5.4 million in his final year. Florida PSC filings (2023, case 00793-2023) reference Silagy's retirement in the context of ongoing controversy.
Securities Fraud Lawsuit — Filed, Dismissed, Revived — 2023–2025
The City of Hollywood Police Officers pension fund and other investors filed a securities fraud class action against NextEra/FPL, alleging the company made misleading public statements about the ghost candidate connections and related political activities. A federal district judge dismissed the case. The 11th Circuit Court of Appeals revived it in November 2025 — ruling the plaintiffs had adequately alleged securities fraud claims. The lawsuit is now active again. Paul Weiss (NextEra's law firm) had previously secured a dismissal with prejudice that the appeals court overturned. Clean Virginia's May 2026 statement notes NextEra "recently settled a securities fraud class action lawsuit" — though this conflicts with the 11th Circuit revival; one of these references may involve a separate matter. Both are documented in published sources.
Dark Money Networks — Documented by Floodlight News and The Guardian
The Guardian (July 2022) reported on leaked documents showing U.S. power companies, including NextEra/FPL, secretly spending millions through dark money networks to stop clean energy competition and influence state regulatory bodies. Floodlight News documented that some of the same actors in the Florida ghost candidate scandal were also involved in dark money spending in the 2020 federal elections. An investigation by Floodlight and Capital B documented how utility companies, including those in NextEra's network, routed money through Black civil rights organizations to build political cover.
Florida Customer Rate Impact — The Pattern Clean Virginia Is Warning Virginia About
Clean Virginia's May 18, 2026 statement: "FPL customers in Florida are experiencing what opponents have called the largest rate hike in American history. Recent research showed that Florida Power & Light's profits in 2025 made up a whopping 27.4% of a customer's electric bill — one of the highest ratios in the country and more than 13 percentage points higher than the national average." NextEra projects 11% annual rate base growth through 2032 for the combined Dominion/NextEra entity. Because utilities earn profit on their rate base, rapid rate base growth = higher long-term bills. The $2.25 billion one-time credit to Virginia customers is a one-time payment — not a reduction in the ongoing profit rate (ROE) that determines your monthly bill forever.

The University of Illinois Center for Secure Water — January 2026

A peer-reviewed analysis published January 30, 2026 by the University of Illinois Center for Secure Water confirmed the following — this is the most authoritative academic source found on this issue:

  • Virginia data centers consumed approximately 2.1 billion gallons in 2023 — 86% more than in 2019
  • Most existing and planned data centers sit within already water-constrained watersheds — specifically the Potomac, Rappahannock, North Anna, and James
  • Data center water use is "among the least transparent components" of Virginia's water system — facility-level withdrawals are not standardized or publicly disclosed
  • The current permit-by-permit approach means cumulative stress is invisible until thresholds are already crossed
  • Indirect water use from electricity generation (Scope 2) accounts for roughly two-thirds of total data center water footprint — the direct cooling figure is only part of the real number
  • New hyperscale AI facilities operate at 100–1,000 MW power capacity — comparable to 80,000 to 800,000 homes' electricity demand
The Transparency Gap Is Documented By Academics: The University of Illinois paper specifically states: "data centers remain among the least transparent industrial water users. Reporting is typically partial, aggregated, and non-standardized." This is why individual permit applications don't show the cumulative picture — and why the FOIA requests and water disclosure legislation (SB 553/HB 496) are so important.
Sources: JLARC 2024 Data Centers in Virginia, Report 598 (jlarc.virginia.gov/pdfs/reports/Rpt598.pdf); University of Illinois Center for Secure Water, "Data Center Expansion in Virginia: Closing Critical Gaps for Informed Water Planning and Permitting," January 30, 2026 (securewater.illinois.edu); Potomac Conservancy citing JLARC; Fredericksburg Free Press Nov 11, 2024.

The Core Conflict: The DEQ required Caroline County to project business water needs 30 years forward as part of the permitting process — which forced data center commercial growth into the permit application. Supervisor Sili (Bowling Green District) argues this doesn't mean those businesses depend on the permit — "approved and by-right data centers will be serviced by county water which lies in a different aquifer." Stuart's counter: the permit language as written specifically mentions shifting water to Carmel Church "for data centers," which is why he opposes it in its current form. The residential water need is real and urgent. The commercial data center need is what's blocking it.
