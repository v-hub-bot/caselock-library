☠️ Google Data Center — R410A Refrigerant Leak
July 16, 2025
📍 Pryor, Oklahoma · MidAmerica Industrial Park
A major R410A refrigerant leak at Google's Pryor facility — its second-largest data center in the world — affected approximately 60 GardaWorld security contractors. Workers are now reporting memory loss, Parkinson's-like tremors, and supplemental oxygen dependency. When asked for internal safety reports, Google's legal team objected and refused to produce documents.

The chemistry of R410A: This refrigerant is heavier than air — it sinks and pools, displacing breathable oxygen at floor level. When it contacts heat sources (like server racks or electrical components), it decomposes into Hydrofluoric Acid and Carbonyl Halides — related to phosgene gas, which was used as a chemical weapon in World War I. Inhaling concentrated fluorocarbons causes "solvent-induced neurotoxicity." When the brain is simultaneously starved of oxygen and exposed to high fluorocarbon levels, it damages the basal ganglia — the brain structure that controls movement.
Status: Google blocking document production. Workers impacted long-term. Community organizing at Oklahoma Data Center Accountability group. NDAs reportedly being used to silence affected workers.
🔥 Moss Landing BESS Fire — 55,000 Lbs of Toxic Metals Released
January 16, 2025
📍 Moss Landing, California (Monterey County) · Vistra Energy Facility
The world's largest battery energy storage facility caught fire and burned for multiple days. The fire released approximately 55,000 pounds of toxic metals into one of California's most sensitive wetlands. Soil sampling found toxic metal levels up to 1,000 times higher than normal up to one mile from the facility.

Schools closed. Major roads shut down. Agricultural operations halted. The fire re-ignited February 18 — a full month later. Toxic smoke blanketed Monterey County and beyond, depositing heavy metals in wetlands and the ocean. Litigation is active via Singleton Schreiber.
Chemicals released: Hydrogen fluoride, heavy metals (lithium, cobalt, nickel, manganese), particulate matter. Litigation ongoing. Community health monitoring required.

The Evaporation Problem Nobody Talks About

Of the 1.1 billion gallons Google used in Pryor in one year, only 253 million gallons returned to the river as discharge. The remaining 847 million gallons evaporated — permanently removed from the local watershed. This is called "consumptive use." Unlike discharge (which at least stays in the watershed), evaporated water is simply gone — it may fall as precipitation hundreds of miles away, or not at all.

🔊 PART 4: Noise, Air Quality & Health Effects

🔊
Noise levels from industrial diesel generators can reach 105 decibels — as loud as a jet aircraft flying overhead (EESI). Cooling fans and HVAC systems add a constant low-frequency baseline. The combination creates documented health effects in nearby communities: sleep disruption, cardiovascular stress, anxiety, and infrasound-related symptoms that residents describe as a constant "hum" or "vibration" they feel more than hear.

Documented Noise Sources at Hyperscale Data Centers

🔊 Diesel Generators: 105 dB peak during load testing. Most hyperscale campuses run monthly no-load tests on all generators. A campus with 300+ generators tests them systematically — creating weeks of elevated noise each year. Normal conversation is 60 dB. OSHA requires hearing protection above 85 dB. At 105 dB, safe exposure is under 1 minute.

🌀 Cooling Systems: Industrial HVAC and cooling towers run 24/7/365. Low-frequency components penetrate walls and travel long distances. At frequencies below 20 Hz (infrasound), sound travels miles and passes through structures as vibration. Multiple peer-reviewed studies document cardiac effects from infrasound exposure above 100 dB within one hour (US National Library of Medicine).

💨 Diesel Exhaust (Air Quality): Each generator run produces NOx, PM2.5 (fine particulate), benzene, and formaldehyde. A 300-generator campus running monthly tests generates cumulative emissions that, in many jurisdictions, would trigger Title V Clean Air Act permitting requirements — but most data centers avoid this by staying just below individual permit thresholds while operating collectively above them. Virginia DEQ Tier 4 engine rules (effective July 2026) are the first attempt to address this cumulatively.

🌡️ Thermal Pollution: Cooling tower discharge water is significantly warmer than source water. Returning warmer water to rivers and streams stresses aquatic ecosystems. In Oklahoma, Google's 253M gallons of annual discharge returns to the Neosho River at elevated temperatures. Long-term ecological impacts on fish spawning and aquatic life are not studied or regulated at the permit level.

Legionella — The Documented Outbreak Risk

⚠️ Oklahoma City metro, 2025: At least two facilities in the Oklahoma City area were found with Legionella bacteria in their cooling systems (KOCO 5 News). Cooling towers are a documented Legionella breeding ground when water temperatures are in the 77–108°F range and the towers aren't properly maintained. Data centers with industrial cooling towers must be required to maintain and disclose Legionella monitoring records. Most don't have to publicly report.

Sources: EESI · US National Library of Medicine (cardiac effects) · KOCO 5 News (Legionella) · Science Direct 2025 · Virginia DEQ Tier 4 rules 2026

⚖️ PART 7: Legal Tools — What Applies in Your State

FEDERAL · CLEAN WATER ACT
33 U.S.C. § 1365 — Citizen Suit Provision
Any citizen can sue a data center that discharges water without an NPDES permit, violates its permit conditions, or fails to meet effluent standards. Does not require you to be personally harmed — just that a violation occurred. Fee-shifting provision: if you win, the company pays your attorneys' fees.
FEDERAL · SAFE DRINKING WATER ACT
42 U.S.C. § 300j-8 — Citizen Enforcement
Allows citizens to sue violators of SDWA standards. Applies if a data center's water withdrawal or discharge activities contaminate a public water supply or a source aquifer. Relevant for R410A decomposition products (hydrogen fluoride, carbonyl halides) reaching groundwater.
FEDERAL · CLEAN AIR ACT
42 U.S.C. § 7661 — Title V Permitting Threshold
Facilities emitting 100 tons/year of NOx or PM2.5 require a Title V major source permit with full public notice and comment. Data centers often avoid this by counting individual generators, not the campus total. Demand your state air agency calculate cumulative campus-wide emissions, not unit-by-unit.
FEDERAL · CERCLA / SUPERFUND
42 U.S.C. § 9607 — Hazardous Substance Liability
If a data center chemical incident (refrigerant, battery fire, diesel spill) contaminates soil or groundwater, CERCLA strict liability attaches. The owner is liable regardless of fault. Applies to R410A decomposition products, lithium battery debris, and diesel spills. Citizen groups can petition EPA for emergency response action.
FEDERAL · RIGHT TO KNOW
42 U.S.C. § 11001 — EPCRA / SARA Title III
The Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act requires facilities storing threshold quantities of hazardous chemicals to report to Local Emergency Planning Committees (LEPCs). R410A, diesel fuel, battery chemicals, and halon may all meet thresholds. FOIA your LEPC for what your local data center has reported. If they haven't filed, that itself is a violation.
STATE TOOL · ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW
SEPA / NEPA-equivalent state laws (40+ states)
Most states have environmental review requirements triggered by large infrastructure projects. A hyperscale data center drawing millions of gallons from a stressed aquifer and requiring new power transmission likely triggers a mandatory environmental impact statement. If the agency skipped this, the permit is legally challengeable — this is exactly what voided the $40B Texas project.
STATE TOOL · WATER RIGHTS
Prior Appropriation (Western States) / Riparian Rights (Eastern States)
In prior appropriation states (AZ, NV, TX, CO, OK, UT), water rights are "first in time, first in right." Existing agricultural, residential, and municipal users have senior rights to data centers, which are new applicants. Challenge: demand your state water agency document where a new data center's water rights rank in the priority system relative to existing users.
FEDERAL · NDAA SECTION 889
National Defense Authorization Act § 889 (2019, extended)
Federal contractors and grant recipients are prohibited from using Huawei, ZTE, Hikvision, Dahua, or Hytera equipment. Any data center holding federal cloud contracts must certify compliance. You can demand this certification as a condition of local permit approval for facilities seeking federal contracts. Applicable in combination with E.O. 14318 (2025).
Sources: US Code · EPA · Congressional Research Service · CaseLock legal research

Step 1 — Know What You're Fighting

🔍 Identify which type: Is it Enterprise (1 tenant, smaller), Colocation (multiple tenants, medium), or Hyperscale/AI (industrial campus, hundreds of MW)? The type determines what legal tools apply and how hard the fight will be.

📋 FOIA immediately: Request all permit applications, environmental assessments, water withdrawal permits, air quality permits, and chemical storage notifications from your county and state environmental agency. Do this before construction starts — it's 10x harder after.

Step 2 — Document Your Community Impact

💧 Water: Document your water pressure, taste, smell, color. Request water quality test results from your utility. Check if your private well is near the facility. Before/after photos of streams and waterways matter.

🔊 Noise: Download a free dB meter app. Document noise levels at different times and distances. Keep a log with dates, times, weather conditions, and readings. This becomes evidence for a noise ordinance challenge or nuisance lawsuit.

💨 Air: Document generator test days — note dates, duration, smell. Check your state's air quality monitoring network for your ZIP code. Request DEQ/EPA air quality monitoring data near the facility.

📈 Utility bills: Keep every bill. Document rate increases. Cross-reference with the date new data centers came online in your utility's service area.

Step 3 — Organize

🤝 Connect with existing national networks: "Say NO to Data Centers" (Facebook group — the one in your screenshots). Virginia LCV (valcv.org). Good Jobs First (goodjobsfirst.org — data center accountability). Earthjustice (earthjustice.org — environmental litigation). Data Center Bans tracker (datacenterbans.com).

🏛️ Show up to public meetings: Board of Supervisors. Planning Commission. State Corporation Commission hearings. Every speaker who shows up with documented evidence matters. Elected officials respond to constituent presence — especially when documented evidence is on the table.

Step 4 — Use the Legal Tools

⚖️ Clean Water Act citizen suit: If there's an unpermitted discharge or permit violation, file a 60-day notice letter to the company and EPA/state agency. This is a prerequisite to filing a citizen suit — and many companies settle after receiving the notice letter alone.

📄 EPCRA right-to-know: Request your LEPC's records on what hazardous chemicals the data center has reported. If they haven't filed, report the violation to EPA. This is completely free and takes 30 minutes.

🗺️ Demand three-tier zoning: Push your Board of Supervisors to distinguish Enterprise from Colocation from Hyperscale in your zoning ordinance. South Dakota just gave local governments this authority by state law — use that model.
