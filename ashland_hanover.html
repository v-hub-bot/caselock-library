🏗️ Tract's 1,200-Acre Hanover Technology Park — Every Verified Detail

The First and Largest Approval — March 27, 2024 — Unanimous Board Vote Over Massive Community Opposition

The Developer: Tract

Tract is a Denver, Colorado-based land acquisitions company specializing specifically in data center developments. They acquired 1,200 acres of land along Hickory Hill Road, east of I-95 in Hanover County's Beaverdam District. Tract's own project page confirms the site is now "zoned for by-right data center use" with "an existing utility infrastructure" on site. At full build, Tract's Hanover Technology Park is targeted for up to 2.4 gigawatts of power capacity — equivalent to the electricity demand of approximately 1.9 million homes. Target energization date: 2028.

What Was Approved — Verified Numbers

  • 1,200 acres — Beaverdam District, east of I-95, south side of Hickory Hill Road, north of Ashland
  • Up to 2.4 GW power capacity at full build (Tract / GRPVA.com)
  • 700,000 gallons of water per day reserved for the site — confirmed in the conditional approval (VPM News March 2024)
  • Approximately 30 buildings of various heights — multiple data center campuses on the same site
  • Around 800 employees on site at full operation — the stated permanent job count (VPM March 2024)
  • Multiple three-megawatt backup generators plus lithium battery storage systems and diesel fuel storage
  • Timmons Group (Richmond-based design firm) produced the conceptual site plan

Developer Commitments Made to Get Approval

  • $3.5 million for Hickory Hill Road improvements
  • Improvements at Elletts Crossing and Route 1
  • Funds to address potential road damage from construction traffic
  • Access road to the Hickory Hill African American Slave Cemetery and Ashley Farm — both adjacent to the site
  • Paid training for emergency services and first responders for data center-specific calls
  • $100,000 to the county for Washington Lacy Park improvements
  • Natural sound buffers and landscaping exceeding county requirements
The cemetery and Ashley Farm: The Hickory Hill African American Slave Cemetery is physically adjacent to the 1,200-acre data center site. This is a documented heritage site. The access road commitment was a condition of approval — not a voluntary concession. McKey Berkman, whose family has owned the adjacent 250-plus-acre farm for generations and whose ancestors are buried there, opposed the project directly at the Board of Supervisors hearing. The Board approved it anyway unanimously.

The Vote — March 27, 2024

Three unanimous measures: rezoning, conditional-use permit, and special exceptions. Every Board member voted yes. The vote occurred despite what resident Doug Boardman described as more public opposition than any project he had witnessed in his two decades in the district.

"The 'nos' to 'yeses' at every meeting are vastly in opposition. So, your constituents do not want this. Please consider your residents, your constituents and deny this proposal. You don't get a do-over here. Please do not leave this data center as your legacy."
— Doug Boardman, Beaverdam District resident of 20+ years, Board of Supervisors hearing, March 27, 2024 (VPM News)
"I know this project has brought a lot of passion and a lot of emotion, and there's a substantial number of our citizens that are in the Beaverdam District that would prefer a project like this did not come into our area. At some point, we're going to have to make a call… I think I'm doing the best that I can for Hanover County, because I see the needs of 110,000 citizens, and not just a few."
— Beaverdam District Supervisor Jeff Stoneman, justifying his yes vote, March 27, 2024 (VPM News)
"This project scared me when I first saw it. A brand new supervisor having to handle something like this and try to make these kinds of decisions is very intimidating… It would have been very easy for me to just vote 'no,' and let the other supervisors carry the water for me and avoid that political fallout, but I'm not that kind of guy."
— Supervisor Jeff Stoneman, after the vote, March 27, 2024 (VPM News)
Source: VPM News, "Hanover supervisors approve 1,200-acre data center development," Lyndon German, March 28, 2024 (vpm.org/news/2024-03-28/hanover-supervisors-data-center-tract-beaverdam); Tract project page (tract.com/project/hanover/); GRPVA.com "Tract approved for 1200-acre data center campus in Hanover"; Baxtel.com Tract Hanover County data center entry.
🚨 Mountain Road Tech Park — THE FIGHT STILL OPEN

430 Acres. Developer: Tract. Planning Commission 6-1 Recommendation. Board of Supervisors Vote STILL NOT TAKEN.

The Mountain Road Technology Park is Tract's second Hanover County project — a proposed master-planned data center campus along Route 33 / Mountain Road in western Hanover County. The site spans more than 430 acres — roughly the size of 325 football fields. The project requires zoning approval from the Board of Supervisors.

The Planning Commission Vote — April 2026

The planning commission voted 6 in favor, 1 against. The lone dissenting vote was Ashland Commissioner Alan Abbott — the Ashland district representative. His opposition is on the record.

Abbott's concern, as reported by VPM News: "Right [here]" — he pointed directly to the impact on the Ashland community. His vote is significant because the Mountain Road site is in western Hanover, closer to the town of Ashland than the Hickory Hill Road site.

This is the Hanover community's Festus moment. The Board of Supervisors vote has not been taken. The planning commission's recommendation is not binding — the Board decides. Every Hanover County voter who opposed this project needs to be at the Board of Supervisors hearing for Mountain Road Tech Park and on record. Ellen Scott's comment — "Haven't seen any tax relief from the data center they approved outside of Ashland. It's time for new representatives" — is the correct framing. The Tract 1,200-acre campus was approved in March 2024. It has not yet been energized (target: 2028). The promised $1.78B over 20 years has not materialized because it takes years. Now a second Tract campus is coming before the same Board that approved the first one unanimously.

What the Mountain Road Tech Park Developer Committed (per mountainroadtechpark.com)

The project has its own official website (mountainroadtechpark.com) — a PR tool confirming the project is real and actively being developed.

No decommissioning plan: Community member testimony at a Board of Supervisors meeting (documented in Facebook group "Hanover data center project lacks decommissioning plan") specifically raised the absence of a decommissioning plan for the Mountain Road and Tradeport data centers. What happens when the facility closes — who pays to remove the buildings, equipment, and infrastructure? This is an unresolved question that belongs in every public comment at the hearing.
Sources: Richmond BizSense April 17, 2026 (Hanover Planning Commission recommends approval of 430-acre project); VPM News April 17, 2026 (6-1 vote, Abbott the lone no); WRIC local news (planning commission advances proposal); mountainroadtechpark.com (developer site); Facebook group community testimony on decommissioning (2026).

The Ashland water system: The Town of Ashland has its own water system separate from Hanover County's. The 700,000 gallons per day Tract reservation draws from county water infrastructure. Any data center in the Mountain Road area — which is in western Hanover, closer to Ashland — creates additional demand on a shared watershed. The specific water source and Ashland's water system capacity relative to data center demand has not been publicly disclosed — this is exactly what SB553/HB496 (the water disclosure bills currently in Virginia General Assembly conference) would require.

📅 Complete Timeline — Verified from Named Sources

How Hanover County Became a Data Center Corridor

2007
Hanover County identifies the Hickory Hill Road parcels as suitable for economic growth/job center in its comprehensive plan. A corporate decision made 17 years before the community found out what it meant.
2017
Comprehensive plan amended — site continues to be designated as economic development area.
2024 — early
Tract acquires 1,200 acres along Hickory Hill Road, east of I-95. Plans submitted for rezoning, conditional use permit, and special exceptions for a data center campus.
March 27, 2024
Hanover County Board of Supervisors unanimously approves Tract's 1,200-acre Hanover Technology Park — three separate measures — over significant community opposition. Beaverdam District Supervisor Jeff Stoneman casts the deciding yes vote despite acknowledging community opposition. Same night: separate 400-acre proposal is withdrawn.
December 2024
Board of Supervisors unanimously REJECTS the 131-acre Iron Horse Business Park data center at the Hanover-Ashland border (Ashland Road). One rejection among multiple approvals — the community notes the inconsistency.
Early 2026
Tract submits application for Mountain Road Technology Park — 430+ acres on Route 33/Mountain Road in western Hanover County. Multiple planning commission meetings held. Hours of community testimony at each hearing.
April 17, 2026
Hanover County Planning Commission votes 6-1 to recommend approval of Mountain Road Tech Park. Lone dissenting vote: Ashland Commissioner Alan Abbott. Recommendation sent to Board of Supervisors.

⚔️ What Hanover/Ashland Residents Can Do Right Now

The Mountain Road Tech Park Board Vote Is the Opportunity. Here Is the Playbook.

🔴 URGENT — Before the Board Vote

  • Find out when the Board of Supervisors will vote on Mountain Road Tech Park. Contact Hanover County government: (804) 365-6000. The planning commission recommendation has been made — the Board vote is the final decision point.
  • Attend the Board of Supervisors hearing and testify. Use the scripts in the "How to Argue This" page — specifically the water volume disclosure argument and the disproportionate burden framing. Ask specifically about the water volume committed per day for Mountain Road Tech Park.
  • Submit written comments before the hearing deadline. Written comments entered into the record become part of the public record for any future appeal. Email and USPS to the Hanover County Board of Supervisors and Planning Department.
  • Ask about the decommissioning plan on the record. No decommissioning plan has been filed. Ask who pays to remove the buildings and infrastructure when the facility closes in 20-30 years.
  • Document every Board member's vote on every data center project. Supervisor Jeff Stoneman (Beaverdam) voted yes on Tract in 2024. His next election is an accountability moment.

🟡 FOIA Targets — Get the Documents

  • FOIA the conditional use permit for the Tract campus — it specifies exact water volumes, generator specifications, and all conditions Tract committed to. Hanover County FOIA officer.
  • FOIA the water source agreement — where does the 700,000 gallons per day come from? Which aquifer, which surface water source, and what is the capacity margin?
  • FOIA all communications between county staff and Tract from 2023-2024, before the first application was filed. See how early the deal was being structured before the public knew.
  • Request the economic impact study that produced the $1.78 billion 20-year projection — who commissioned it? Was it Tract's own consultant or an independent analysis?
  • Look up Tract's LLC filings in the Virginia SCC Clerk's system (scc.virginia.gov/clk) to understand the full corporate structure behind "Hanover Technology Park LLC" or equivalent entity names.

🟢 Electoral Accountability — The Festus Model

  • Identify every Board of Supervisors member who voted for the Tract 1,200-acre campus. The vote was unanimous — every member is on record.
  • Determine election dates for each supervisor. Virginia BOS terms are 4 years. Supervisors elected in November 2023 face reelection in November 2027.
  • Start the electoral accountability infrastructure now. Document every vote, every official statement, every commitment made and whether it was kept. Ellen Scott has already publicly stated: "It's time for new representatives." That's the beginning of a campaign.
  • Join Stop Hanover Data Centers — they have national reach (5.8K likes on a single post) and are already mobilized.
  • Connect with the Virginia Data Center Reform Coalition. pecva.org/datacenters — they have 50+ member organizations and legislative relationships in Richmond. Hanover is exactly the kind of case they need documented.
