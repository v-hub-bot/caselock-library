The 85% Evaporation Rule — The Number They Won't Show You

HOW DATA CENTER COOLING ACTUALLY WORKS:

Cooling towers work by intentional evaporation — water is turned to vapor to carry heat away from servers. That vapor rises into the atmosphere and joins the regional weather system. It does not fall back into Lockport's aquifer. It may fall as rain hundreds of miles away.

Up to 85% of every gallon withdrawn from your aquifer evaporates permanently.

A single mid-size data center: 3,000,000 gallons/day withdrawn → 2,550,000 gallons/day gone forever → 930 million gallons per year permanently removed from an aquifer Lockport is already abandoning.

The "we return water to the system" claim is technically true — but the system it returns to is the atmosphere, not your aquifer.
Source: Missouri Policy Initiative water study; Energy and Environment Study Institute (EESI)

The "Cone of Depression" — What Happens to Your Neighbors' Wells

When a large facility pumps massive groundwater volumes, it creates a funnel-shaped drop in the surrounding water table — pulling private wells and municipal intakes dry within a 1–2 mile radius. In Lockport, where the aquifer is under documented terminal stress, this effect is amplified. Wells go dry within weeks or months. Drilling deeper costs homeowners $10,000–$25,000 out of pocket. No developer has any obligation to reimburse you.

⚠️ THE NEWTON COUNTY, GA LESSON: A Georgia data center was approved without a pre-approval groundwater baseline study. Wells went dry after construction. Residents had no legal recourse because without a baseline, they could not prove the data center caused the drop. Demand a public baseline groundwater study BEFORE any permit is issued in Lockport. This is the single most important legal protection you can demand tonight.

Every large data center operates banks of diesel-powered backup generators that are tested regularly — typically monthly — for 30 minutes to several hours at full load. These are not emergency-only devices. They are maintained operational systems that produce continuous emissions every test cycle.

SITE CONTAMINATION — THE REFINERY LEGACY

☣️ The Star Innovation District Is a Former Oil Refinery — Ask the Hard Questions

The Texaco/Chevron refinery operated at 301 W. 2nd Street, Lockport from approximately 1910 to 1981. The site produced aviation gasoline, lubricating oils, and petroleum products for over 70 years. Illinois Pollution Control Board records document contamination including metals, volatile organic compounds (VOCs), and semi-volatile organic compounds (SVOCs).

The City's own master plan document (2022) states: "Historic use of the site as an oil refinery caused contamination of the soil and water on the property." The Herald-News (May 7, 2026) states the site "has been fully environmentally remediated."

⚠️ "Fully remediated" does not mean what you think it means. In Illinois environmental law, "remediation" means contamination has been reduced to levels approved for the intended land use — in this case, industrial. Levels considered safe for industrial use are significantly higher than levels considered safe for residential use or for groundwater protection. A data center is a heavy industrial facility — but it sits adjacent to residential neighborhoods and draws from a community water aquifer. Ask for the actual remediation reports.

What to Request and Demand

  • The complete Illinois EPA remediation records for the Star Innovation District site — including the final "No Further Remediation" (NFR) letter, if one exists, and its conditions
  • The specific land-use restrictions in the NFR letter — industrial-only remediation has deed restrictions that prohibit residential use and may limit groundwater extraction
  • A new Phase II Environmental Site Assessment specific to data center construction — vibration from construction can disturb remediated soil and release legacy contaminants
  • Groundwater monitoring data from the site's post-remediation period

ILLINOIS STATE LAW — YOUR LEGAL WEAPONS

⚖️ What Illinois Law Actually Gives Lockport

KEY FACT: Lockport is a home-rule municipality (population over 25,000). Under Article VII, §6(a) of the Illinois Constitution, home-rule units have broader authority than non-home-rule towns — they can enact regulations exceeding state minimums without special legislative approval. Lockport has more legal power here than most Illinois towns. The question is whether the City Council is willing to use it.
Illinois Municipal Code
Full Local Zoning Authority
65 ILCS 5/11-13-1 through 5/11-13-25

Illinois municipalities have broad, independent authority to regulate land use through zoning ordinances — including classifying, restricting, or prohibiting specific industrial uses. The Star Innovation District is currently zoned for data center use per the 2022 master plan — but zoning classifications can be amended. That amendment requires a public hearing and a City Council vote.

WILL COUNTY & CITY LAW — LOCAL AUTHORITY

🏛️ Will County and Lockport — Local Legal Pressure Points

Special Use Permit — The Most Important Gate
Two Veto Points: ZBA + City Council
City of Lockport Zoning Code — heavy industrial uses require SUP

Data centers classified as heavy industrial require a Special Use Permit (SUP). The SUP process requires: (1) a formal application, (2) public notice to all property owners within 250–500 feet, (3) a public hearing before the Zoning Board of Appeals where residents can testify, and (4) a separate vote by the full City Council. This creates two distinct veto points. Pack both hearings with organized opposition. Pekin, IL won at exactly this stage.

MOST CRITICAL STAGE
Annexation Agreement — The Hidden Trap
How Deals Get Locked In Before You Know
65 ILCS 5/11-15.1 — Illinois Municipal Annexation Authority

If any portion of the development area is not yet formally within city limits, a developer may seek an annexation agreement — a contract bundling zoning, tax incentives, and infrastructure commitments. Annexation agreements require a public hearing AND a supermajority Council vote. They lock in the terms before a standard SUP hearing occurs. File a FOIA request specifically asking: "Has any annexation agreement or letter of intent been executed or is one pending for the Star Innovation District?"

FILE FOIA NOW

willcounty.gov/County-Offices/Economic-Development/Will-County-Land-Use-Department

County infrastructure permits — roads, stormwater, utilities — may be required for large-scale development even within city limits. Contact the Will County Land Use Department to document all pending or anticipated county-level permits and create a parallel public record at the county level.

Ongoing — The Special Use Permit Hearing Is The Real Battle

  • When a SUP application is filed, you will receive legal notice — every property owner within the notification radius must be formally served
  • The SUP public hearing before the Zoning Board of Appeals is where organized, documented testimony wins — this is the Pekin model
  • Submit formal written comments to the City Clerk before every hearing — these become part of the permanent administrative record
  • If the City attempts to skip required steps or railroad a decision — contact an Illinois environmental attorney immediately about a procedural challenge under the Open Meetings Act and Administrative Procedure Act
Illinois Environmental Law support: Environmental Law & Policy Center — elpc.org | (312) 673-6500. Also: Land of Lincoln Legal Aid for referrals.
If a permit is approved and you believe proper process was not followed: you have 35 days to file an administrative appeal under Illinois law. Document every procedural step now so you have the record if needed.
